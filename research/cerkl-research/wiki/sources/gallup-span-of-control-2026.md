# Span of Control: What's the Optimal Team Size for Managers?

**Author/Publisher:** Jim Harter / [[gallup]] | **Published:** 2026-01-14 | **Source type:** Analyst research report | **URL:** https://www.gallup.com/workplace/700718/span-control-optimal-team-size-managers.aspx

## Summary

Gallup's flagship 2026 piece on the "Great Flattening" — orgs widening manager spans and removing middle layers. Backed by a 16,442-manager US panel (2022–2024) and a meta-analysis of 92,252 teams across 46 countries. Headline takeaway: **manager talent and meaningful weekly feedback matter more than span size**. Pressure-tests the "flatten everything" wave by showing engagement collapses when low-talent managers run large teams, and triples when employees get weekly meaningful feedback regardless of span. The single most-citable IC-relevant stat from the piece is the **median-vs-mean gap**: the *average* US span is 12.1 reports per manager in 2025, but the *median* is still 6 — a long-tail problem, not a universal one.

## Key Takeaways

- **Median ≠ mean.** Average US span = 12.1 (2025); median ≈ 6. **13% of managers oversee 25+; 37% oversee fewer than 5.** Cite the median, not the mean, in any positioning aimed at SMB/Foundations buyers — most managers' lived experience is still small-team. This is the load-bearing nuance the headline-stat consumers miss.
- **Manager talent trumps span.** Highly talented managers can lead 25+ reports and stay engaged. Medium/low talent managers' engagement falls off with even moderate-size teams. Argues against any vendor or consultant pitch that treats span as the primary lever.
- **Weekly meaningful feedback is the single highest-leverage habit.** Triples the engaged-employee rate (~70% engaged when employees strongly agree they receive it weekly, vs ~25% when they don't). **Only 16% of employees report their last manager conversation was extremely meaningful.** A massive opportunity gap that pairs with [[manager-cascade]]'s 56/4 gap (priority vs. effectiveness).
- **97% of US managers also do individual-contributor work. Median = 40% of time on non-managerial tasks.** When IC-work share crosses 40%, engagement drops sharply — especially with larger teams. This is the *time-allocation* angle on Karian's "structurally impossible" frame.
- **Hybrid/remote managers face engagement challenges as team size grows, regardless of talent.** On-site managers don't. Implies the hybrid-coordination tax (Karian's root cause #2) is real and Gallup-corroborated.

## Notable Claims

- **Time-series (per Gallup Manager Panel):** 2013 = 10.9 reports per manager; 2024 = 10.9; 2025 = 12.1. Sample sizes: n=2,402 (2013) → n=8,795 (2025).
- **⚠️ Provenance flag.** Gallup's article states "this represents nearly a 50% increase since 2013," but the time series shown (10.9 → 10.9 → 12.1 = +11% over 12 years, with the entire jump in 2024–2025) does not support that headline. Most likely the "+50%" refers to the share of teams with 25+ employees (which rose two percentage points) — but as written in the article the statistic is at best ambiguous. Re-source via Gallup's underlying methodology appendix before citing the +50% figure in any blog post or deck. **Conservative citation:** "Gallup's manager panel shows U.S. average span rose from 10.9 (2013/2024) to 12.1 (2025) — driven by a tail of mega-teams, not a universal shift."
- **Distribution snapshot (2025):** 37% oversee <5 | 66% oversee <10 | 22% oversee 10–24 | 13% oversee 25+.
- "Median team size remains steady at approximately five to six employees per manager."
- "Highly engaged teams of 12 or more workers who are supported by effective management — double the current median of six workers per team — can thrive, while poorly managed teams struggle."
- "Manager talent trumps span of control."
- "In today's workplace, where employees feel increasingly disconnected from their organizations, managers' open communication with employees is more important than the number of direct reports."
- BLS reference: ~1 manager per 11.5 employees (US baseline).
- 97% of US managers do some IC work; median 40% of time on IC tasks; >40% IC-time managers' engagement drops sharply.
- Weekly meaningful feedback → ~70% engaged regardless of team size. Without it → ~25% engaged. **3x engagement multiplier.**
- 16% of employees say their last manager conversation was "extremely meaningful."

## Contradicts

- Partial contradiction with the IC discourse's casual citation of "+50% increase since 2013" — Gallup states the figure but the underlying time series (10.9 → 12.1) doesn't support a 50% mean-span increase. Worth pinning into the [[span-of-control]] concept page so downstream writing has the flag.
- Pressure-tests Karian's "average manager has too many reports" framing — Gallup's median ≈ 6 means most managers' lived experience is *not* the 12.1 average. Karian's structural argument (responsibility without authority, hybrid load, admin bloat, emotional absorption) survives without the +50% stat; the role-design problem doesn't depend on every manager running a huge team.
