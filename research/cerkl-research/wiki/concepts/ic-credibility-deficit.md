# IC Credibility Deficit

## Definition
[[ghassan-karian]]'s thesis that employee distrust of internal communication is *rational and systemic* — IC erodes its own credibility not because comms teams are bad, but because the system pushes them to communicate in three trust-killing ways. Grounded in [[ipsos-karian-and-box]] data across "millions of employee responses."

## Why It Matters
It reframes "low IC trust" from a messaging-quality problem (write better) into a *structural* problem (stop doing three things). That makes it actionable and ties directly to the delivery thesis: trust is a function of *what IC is allowed to say and when*, not of polish ([[delivery-as-comms-strategy]]). It also gives IC a measurable target — candour↔trust is quantifiable in IKB's data.

## How It Works
IC loses credibility when it tries to do three things it should never do:
1. **Protect leaders from discomfort** — euphemism. "Cost-cutting" → "strategic realignment"; "layoff" → "rebalancing of capacity"; "failed initiative" → "valuable learning cycle." (Direct overlap with [[boilerplate-layoff-language]].)
2. **Pretend neutrality while delivering spin** — pre-approved, legally-sanitised messaging where "the truth can no longer be located." Over-curation makes trust plummet.
3. **Communicate on leadership's timetable, not reality's** — bad news late, good news early, context never. By the time IC speaks, employees already heard it from news, WhatsApp, peers, or the rumour network. (The timing axis of [[layoff-comms-choreography]].)

Diagnostic tell: employees describe leadership comms as "scripted, insincere, patronising, cold, evasive."
