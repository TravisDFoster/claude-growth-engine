# AI Washing

## Definition

Three overlapping uses in 2026 IC discourse:

1. **Layoff AI-washing** — attributing workforce cuts to AI when the underlying business driver is something else (cost-cut, restructure, performance management). Most acutely named by Sam Altman (February 2026): *"AI washing where people are blaming AI for layoffs they would otherwise do."*
2. **Disclaimer AI-washing** — explicitly disclaiming an AI driver in a layoff memo (*"this is not an AI optimization"*) in a way that coverage and employees parse as defensive deflection rather than candor — see the [[gitlab]] case in [[ragan-week-in-comms-linkedin-gm-gitlab-2026-05-15]].
3. **Side-channel AI-washing** (2026-05 addition) — official memo deliberately *omits* the AI rationale while a non-CEO executive confirms it through a side channel that press picks up within 48 hours. The [[linkedin]] case in [[layoff-memo-subgenre-2026-05-21]]: Shapero's memo avoids AI; CPO Hari Srinivasan separately tells staff the goal is *"smaller, faster and more agile teams that can heavily leverage AI tools."* The split is the failure.

## Why It Matters

Two reasons this concept earns its own page:

1. **It contaminates the [[people-amplification]] empirical case.** [[gartner]]'s zero-correlation-between-cuts-and-ROI finding implicitly assumes the cuts are AI-driven. If a meaningful share are AI-washed (real driver: cost), then "AI doesn't deliver ROI from cuts" is partially confounded with "AI wasn't actually the reason for the cut." Doesn't break the finding — companies still didn't get ROI — but does add caveat to the analyst frame.
2. **It is now a public anti-pattern in change comms.** Per Ragan's GitLab analysis ([[ragan-week-in-comms-linkedin-gm-gitlab-2026-05-15]]), the *"this is not AI cost-cutting"* disclaimer reads as defensive. Coverage will call it AI-washing anyway. Better: name the AI shift, name the savings reinvestment specifically, name the affected functions by name.

## How It Works

The pattern in 2026 layoff comms:

- **Public AI narrative as cover** — companies invoke AI as the reason for cuts that would have happened anyway (Altman's framing).
- **Disclaimer as backfire** — disclaiming AI driver (*"this is not AI optimization"*) is reverse-engineered by coverage as confirmation.
- **Void-fill failure** — when the company doesn't name a concrete driver, employees and coverage fill the void with "AI is replacing people" (the GM case in [[layoff-comms-choreography]]).

The IC playbook to *avoid* AI-washing:

- **If AI is part of the reason, name it specifically** — which products, which workflows, which roles redirected
- **Name reinvestment plans concretely** — which teams gain headcount, which capabilities the savings fund
- **Don't preempt with disclaimers** — they read as defensive; let the body of the message carry the framing
- **Acknowledge employees as stakeholders before the strategy rationale** — the choreography pattern in [[layoff-comms-choreography]]

## Tensions / Criticisms

- "AI washing" is itself becoming a quick-dismissal frame that can be over-applied — every layoff with an AI mention risks being dismissed as washed. Not every claim is washed.
- The distinction between *real* AI-driven cuts and *AI-washed* cuts is hard to verify externally — companies don't separate the books.
- Disclaimer AI-washing depends on coverage interpretation; a well-crafted disclaimer in a different cultural moment might land. The 2026 climate is unusually skeptical.
