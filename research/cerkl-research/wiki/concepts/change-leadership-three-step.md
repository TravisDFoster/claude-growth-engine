# Change-Leadership Three-Step (Whittle / Gartner)

## Definition

A three-step prescriptive framework for change-leadership communications, articulated by [[mark-whittle]] (VP Advisory, [[gartner]]) in 2025-10 and surfaced via [[ragan-gartner-2026-comms-predictions-2025-10-09]]:

1. **Acknowledge the journey** — recognize constant change as normal and update employees regularly.
2. **Regulate discomfort** — use transparent communication to address employee concerns about transformation.
3. **Train intuition** — build organizational reflexes through comprehensive change communications that help employees see themselves in the narrative.

The framework is short, ordered, and prescriptive — designed to scale through manager training and to be repeated verbatim in C-suite slide decks. Its accompanying verbatim is *"effective leadership today is change leadership."*

## Why It Matters

This is the **practitioner-level pair** to Gartner's organization-level [[people-amplification]] frame. Poitevin's *people-amplification* names what high-ROI AI cohorts *do* (invest more in people); Whittle's three-step names what change leaders *do communicationally* to make that investment land. Together they form the diagnosis-prescription pair Gartner is selling to the CHRO/CCO buyer pair.

For Cerkl, the framework matters because each of its three steps maps to a Cerkl-platform capability gap:

- **"Acknowledge the journey" → regular cadenced delivery.** Cerkl's strength: cadenced send, scheduled cascade, recurring formats.
- **"Regulate discomfort" → audience-aware segmentation.** Cerkl's strength: dynamic segments, MyNews personalization, layered targeting (vs. one-to-many blast).
- **"Train intuition" → repeated narrative across channels and time.** Cerkl's strength: cross-channel delivery + measurement loop showing whether the narrative is actually landing.

The framework is also useful as a **language convergence signal**: analyst-tier vocabulary that practitioners and journalists will repeat unattributed. Cerkl content that adopts this vocabulary will read as analyst-aligned without citing the analyst.

## How It Works

The three steps are sequential but recursive — they're not a campaign sequence ("first step 1, then step 2, then step 3"), they're an *ongoing* posture. Each step assumes the prior is in place but doesn't replace it.

**Mechanism per step:**

- **Acknowledge the journey** — *frequency over event*. The implicit claim: silence between change-moments produces more anxiety than the change-moments themselves. Counter-pattern: leadership comms that only fire at announcement-moments (the GM/Oracle pattern in [[layoff-comms-choreography]]).
- **Regulate discomfort** — *transparency over assurance*. The implicit claim: employees can metabolize discomfort if they understand what's happening; they cannot metabolize uncertainty about what's happening. Counter-pattern: euphemistic "broader organizational change" language ([[boilerplate-layoff-language]]).
- **Train intuition** — *narrative continuity over one-time announcement*. The implicit claim: employees develop an instinctive read on the org's trajectory when the comms cadence is continuous and coherent enough to model. Counter-pattern: episodic high-stakes memos surrounded by silence.

The 2008-09 financial-crisis analogy Whittle uses works as evidence by parallel: in 2008-09, leaders who failed to communicate left employees anxious; the same pattern is repeating in 2026 with AI as the underlying disruption.

## Tensions / Criticisms

- **The framework is descriptive of good-practice, not novel.** Critics will note that "acknowledge / regulate / train" maps onto longstanding change-management vocabulary (Kotter's 8 steps, Prosci's ADKAR). The Gartner contribution is the *naming* and the analyst-tier endorsement, not the underlying insight.
- **Steps 1 and 3 assume comms infrastructure most orgs lack.** "Update employees regularly" and "build organizational reflexes through comprehensive change communications" require an editorial calendar, multi-channel delivery, and a measurement loop. The Workshop/PR Daily 56%/4% manager-cascade gap ([[six-internal-comms-trends-2026]]) is direct counter-evidence that the infrastructure is missing. The framework prescribes what most orgs can't execute.
- **"Regulate discomfort" can read as gaslighting if mishandled.** The phrase implies emotional management *by* leadership; if employees read "regulate" as "suppress," the framework backfires. Cerkl content adopting this language should rephrase as "name the discomfort honestly" rather than "regulate" it.
- **Source authority is one trade-press pickup so far.** The framework appears in one Ragan piece; Gartner's underlying paywalled report (doc 7160430) may or may not enumerate it the same way. Worth watching whether Whittle repeats the three-step in subsequent webinar / press appearances before treating it as a stable Gartner-attributable frame.
