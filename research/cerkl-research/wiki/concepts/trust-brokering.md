# Trust Brokering

## Definition
The [[edelman]] Trust Barometer 2026 frame that employees expect the *employer* (and, operationally, internal communications) to **broker trust** across a workforce — bridging divides, modeling cross-difference collaboration — and that failing to do so carries a measurable productivity cost. Trust is treated as an organizational performance variable, not a soft cultural nicety.

## Why It Matters
It gives IC a mandate beyond message clarity: the function is responsible for *whether collaboration survives differences*. And it supplies the institutional-trust backdrop ([[trust-and-credibility]]) against which [[ic-credibility-deficit]] operates — employers are trusted, but leadership comms must earn its share of that trust.

## How It Works
Key 2026 Barometer data points:
- **78%** trust their employer to do the right thing (highest of any institution); **81%** say the employer is responsible for bridging divides.
- **75% / 44%** gap: employees expect CEOs to bridge trust divides; far fewer think they do it well.
- Trust is turning **insular** — only **22%** trust someone meaningfully different from them; trust grows via openness (49%) and transparency (46%) about differences.
- **Productivity cost:** 42% would put in less effort under a leader with different beliefs; 34% would switch departments to avoid one.
- IC levers employees endorse: shared culture (82%), mixed-values teams (81%), constructive-dialogue training (80%).

Mechanism for IC: trust is "experienced and modeled," not announced — so the work is spotlighting real cross-functional collaboration and constructive disagreement, and promoting dialogue training where it exists.
