# Context Delivery (IC as context, not reputation management)

## Definition
[[ghassan-karian]]'s prescription: credible internal comms treats its job as *delivering context* — helping employees understand and believe what's happening — rather than *managing reputation*. The behavioral name for it is **radical honesty**: tell the truth first, before polish or a carefully-constructed narrative.

## Why It Matters
It's the actionable cure for the [[ic-credibility-deficit]], and it reframes IC's purpose away from PR. The payoff is mobilisation: "mobilisation doesn't come from messaging; it comes from truth." Where comms is experienced this way, leadership-trust confidence in IKB's data rises "into the eighties and nineties" — so it's a performance lever, not a values statement.

## How It Works
Credible IC, per Karian, is: **short, plain, candid, specific, honest about trade-offs, early not late, aligned with lived experience, and willing to say "we got this wrong" or "we don't know yet."**

It is *not*: branded, euphemistic, excessively optimistic, or full of management jargon and slogans.

Sample "adults speaking to adults" messages:
- "This change is painful, but necessary."
- "We handled the last quarter badly; here's what we're fixing."
- "We don't know yet — here's what we're exploring."

Proof point: at [[british-gas]], this approach sustained trust in weekly pulse research even while announcing the offshoring of 3,000 jobs.

## Tensions / Criticisms
- "Radical honesty" collides with legal/comms approval gates that exist for real reasons (securities disclosure, litigation risk). The hard part isn't believing in candour — it's getting permission for it. Karian under-addresses the institutional constraint.
- Risks reading as anti-craft; the point is candour *plus* clarity, not candour instead of skill.
