# 9 lessons from building an AI-integrated comms function

By Paul Gennaro | June 11, 2026

Paul Gennaro is chief communications officer, New York Life, and advisor, Ragan's Center for AI Strategy.

We hosted a Forum Group meeting for more than two dozen corporate communications leaders at New York Life's headquarters in New York City. Unsurprisingly, one of the main topics was AI.

Samantha Stark, Phyusion founder and Center for AI Strategy advisor, walked the group through IPR's 2026 generative AI research, and one element stood out: a nine-item checklist for successful enterprise adoption.

What makes the checklist, which spans culture, leadership, governance and measurement, compelling is completeness and accuracy. After 22 months of integrating AI into corporate communications at New York Life, our experience closely mirrors each element.

The checklist also reinforces a broader point: AI success is less about tools and more about how organizations operate and continuously evolve along with AI.

### 1. Build a strong learning culture

IPR's checklist emphasized structured training, experimentation and peer learning. At New York Life, we went beyond AI 101 sessions, creating an expectation that AI use was part of how we worked, and then tracking and measuring it.

In our team's most recent quarterly AI survey, the number of employees who use AI multiple times a day grew to 90%, reflecting how quickly capability scales when learning is embedded into daily work. That happened because we set an expectation, gave people tools and time, and celebrated what worked.

### 2. Use a human-centric approach

The research highlighted the importance of reinforcing the idea that AI augments, not replaces, human judgment. Our approach has been clear: AI accelerates production, but humans define the final product. That clarity builds confidence, protects quality and enables team member buy-in.

This resonates strongly with the IPR research, which found that most organizations emphasized keeping humans in the loop. For our team, that principle showed up in how we framed AI as infrastructure enabling us to be more strategic, not a threat to creative work.

### 3. Create a unified change narrative

Only 37% of organizations have clearly communicated a change story for AI adoption. Positioning AI within a broader enterprise strategy, at New York Life it is becoming a tech-, data- and AI-powered organization, helped us shift from experimentation to alignment. AI is no longer a novelty. It became part of how we operate.

From a corporate communications perspective, I have been transparent with the team from the beginning: our profession has a finite amount of time to master AI as communicators. We will provide access to the tools and training, encouraging everyone to get involved and grow professionally as individuals and as a team.

### 4. Empower peer learning and champions

The checklist calls for identifying early adopters and amplifying their impact. This has been one of the most powerful accelerators. Informal champions experimenting and sharing created momentum across our team.

When we asked the team what types of training they would like for 2026, 56% identified best practices for creating and updating custom GPTs, Claude Projects and Copilot Agents as a top priority, signaling that we have moved well past the basics and into the territory of building. The lesson: Adoption scales faster through peers than through mandates.

### 5. Engage leadership and model from the top

Leadership engagement is a consistent predictor of adoption success. We have benefited from that at the CEO level, and our corporate communications senior leadership team has modeled it as well.

When I discuss an AI project or GPT I have built, it almost always results in a team member sharing what they have built or learned. That creates momentum and energy.

### 6. Promote responsible, ethical use

Governance remains a central concern. The research highlights risks related to accuracy, bias and compliance. For corporate communications teams, this is not just a risk issue. It is a trust issue.

Clear guardrails, combined with strong human oversight, allow teams to move forward with confidence rather than hesitation. We built an Editorial Guidelines GPT that puts our standards for voice, accuracy and brand integrity into the workflow itself. The guardrails do not slow the work down. They make the work trustworthy.

### 7. Integrate AI into everyday workflows

The real value of AI emerges when it becomes embedded in everyday workflows. Initially, AI might assist with discrete tasks such as drafting emails, summarizing reports or generating outlines. Over time, our team began to rethink the workflow itself. Research synthesis becomes faster. Early drafts become more structured. Time spent on repetitive tasks decreases.

The result is a shift in where communicators focus their time, away from production and toward strategy. AI is not just making work faster. It is changing where we focus, and the impact is measurable. In our Q1 AI survey, 76% of the team reported taking on additional projects due to AI-driven efficiency gains, and 78% reported an increased ability to focus on strategic work.

### 8. Measure for continuous improvement

Metrics should not focus solely on tool usage. They should capture outcomes: time reallocated from drafting to strategic planning, increased capacity to support additional initiatives, improvements in internal client satisfaction and faster turnaround on complex communication needs.

Our approach has been to measure outcomes as well as team engagement. Job satisfaction has increased, with 78% of the team citing our AI implementation as the reason, and our expanded capacity has enabled us to support more initiatives without increasing resources. Measurement connects AI adoption directly to the value that corporate communications delivers to stakeholders.

### 9. Build the right team

The checklist highlights the importance of aligning talent, skills and external expertise. Our team has leaned in. We identified and celebrated early adopters and AI champions. We have five AI "Blue Sky Initiatives" underway with leadership structures for each filled by AI leaders across our team. One team built an agentic system that connects a media-monitoring input directly to Copilot, with our full crisis communications toolkit embedded, so the moment a potential issue surfaces, an email with tailored draft messaging is instantly sent to designated team members. It compresses monitoring, synthesis and drafting into minutes.

We also leverage external learnings from Ragan and its events, such as the annual AI Horizons Summit.

## Observations and lessons learned

Three lessons from the IPR research stand out:

1. Culture matters more than technology. Adoption depends on trust, transparency and leadership example.
2. Workflow integration is the inflection point. Value is created not when individuals use AI, but when teams redesign how work gets done.
3. Discipline remains essential. AI may speed up production, but the standards of accuracy, credibility and brand integrity remain unchanged.

## What comes next

The IPR checklist is valuable not just as a starting point but also as a recurring diagnostic. The organizations that will lead in this space are those that treat it as a living framework they return to quarterly. The next phase of AI adoption will be defined by integration and scale. For corporate communications teams, that means focusing on people, embedding AI into standard workflows while expanding advanced capabilities such as custom agents, strengthening measurement frameworks and continuing to reinforce human judgment as the differentiator.

The lessons detailed in the IPR checklist got us here. As the research makes clear, organizations that will define the future of communications are those that invest in training, governance and experimentation so their teams are ready to use AI confidently, responsibly and creatively, now and in the years ahead.
