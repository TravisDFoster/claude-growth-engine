## Core Message

Benefits communication in 2026 is no longer administrative. It is regulatory, strategic, and measurable.

Three pillars anchor the message:

### Compliance
Pay transparency and benefits regulations require documentation, segmentation, and auditability. "We sent an email" is not a defensible compliance strategy.

### Clarity
One-off emails and static PDFs create confusion. Campaign-based, segmented communication improves understanding and perceived total rewards value.

### Proving It Worked
Open rates are vanity metrics. Leadership expects measurable impact. HR needs engagement data by employee segment to refine messaging and demonstrate effectiveness.

Foundations is positioned as the enterprise-grade internal communications infrastructure that makes this possible for SMBs.

## Matt's Perspective

### Title
**The Cost of Benefits Communications That Nobody's Talking About**

**Supporting line:** Throwing money at rewards and benefits is pointless if you don't invest the same effort in telling people why they should care.

### Synopsis

For too long, benefits communication has been the job nobody owns. Dense policy documents dropped on people during onboarding. Technical summaries sent when someone asks a question. Enrollment guides so long that not even the benefits team reads them.

Getting attention is hard. Helping people understand benefits is harder. Plans are complicated, the subjects are sensitive, and too many benefits are easy to ignore — right up until the moment they're not. Healthcare, retirement savings, income protection are invisible until they're urgent, then they're the most important thing in the world. The cost of people not understanding what they have isn't abstract. It shows up in bad decisions, missed support, and real financial consequences for employees and employers.

Companies pour money into new benefits and wellbeing tools hoping to support people and shift engagement — and then wonder why nothing moves.

Leading employers don't think about "Rewards & Benefits" as a cost line or a compliance requirement but as the most tangible way to bring their Employee Value Proposition to life. It's easy to say you care about your people. A well-communicated benefits package proves it.

If you're still relying on generic email tools, uncoordinated vendor messages, and static PDFs, you're not just creating risk — you're actively undermining the value you're trying to demonstrate and the support you're trying to provide.

Join Matt Frost and Tarek Kamil to cut through the most common pitfalls in benefits communication — and walk away with practical steps to avoid them.

### By Attending, You Will

- **Connect benefits to your EVP:** Pay, rewards, recognition, and wellbeing are the most tangible proof points your Employee Value Proposition has. Learn how to communicate them as a coherent story, not a disconnected feature list.
- **Control the message:** Every single benefit vendor talks directly to your employees. Without oversight it's a completely overwhelming and disconnected experience. Discover how to own the narrative before your providers do it for you.
- **Move from box-ticking compliance to emotion-stirring stories:** Understand what pay transparency legislation and benefits regulation require, and why documentation, segmentation, and auditability matter. But more importantly, learn why ticking the box simply isn't enough anymore.
- **Fight for attention, and cherish it when you have it:** Move beyond one-off email blasts and static PDFs. Learn how a campaign-based, always-on approach improves understanding, drives action, and raises the perceived value of your total rewards.
- **Prove your impact:** We all know that open rates are vanity metrics — but how can we move to more meaningful engagement by employee segment and produce reporting that demonstrates real communication effectiveness to leadership.

Plus, one attendee will receive a Benefits Communication Audit from Matt Frost — a diagnostic review to identify exactly where your company is losing value and what to do about it.

## Polling Questions

**#1. Who primarily owns communication about rewards and benefits in your company?**
- HR / Total Rewards
- Internal Communications
- Individual benefit vendors and providers
- Honestly? Nobody really owns it
- It's shared — but not coordinated

**#2. How confident are you that employees understand the benefits available to them?**
- Very confident … and we have strong data to back it up
- Fairly confident … but it's based on instinct more than evidence
- Not very confident … we hear "I didn't know we had that" too often
- Not at all … our communication is reactive at best

**#3. How do you currently measure whether communications about benefits are working?**
- Open and click rates
- Enrollment numbers and participation data
- Employee survey feedback
- We don't have a reliable way to measure it
- We're expected to prove impact but don't have the tools to do it

**#4. How often do you actively communicate about benefits — outside of open enrollment or a major change?**
- Regularly … we have a planned always-on programme
- Occasionally … when something specific comes up
- Rarely … it's mostly reactive when people ask

**#5. When you do communicate benefits, what does that typically look like?**
- A planned campaign with multiple touchpoints over time
- A single email or announcement
- A document or PDF pushed to a portal
- A mix — but it's not coordinated or consistent
- It depends entirely on who's asking and when
