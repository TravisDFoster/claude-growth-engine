## Section 6 — The four-stage ladder: compliance → clarity → relevance → connection (19:00–26:00)
*[Slide 6: From Compliance to Connection — the four-stage ladder with descriptions]*
*[Slide 7: "FROM COMPLIANCE TO CONNECTION" section divider]*

`18:58` **Matt:** [slide: left-to-right ladder] This is very much a left-to-right piece. Our observation as we work with different organizations all over the world is there's a linear path.

`19:18` **Matt:** **Stage 1 — Compliance.** We communicate because we have to. There are things happening in the UK and the US — all over the world — that we legally have to tell people about. So we start with: we are communicating because we have to. That's the very bare minimum. We said what we had to. Boxes were ticked. Often compliance communication feels like it was written by a lawyer, by a benefits nerd, by someone that just gets off on the details, loves it. That lands with people and they go, "I don't know how to feel about this — it's all very heavy. Feels like we've just churned information out." There are a few clients in the "we just do things because we have to" bare minimum.

`20:14` **Matt:** **Stage 2 — Clarity.** I would say most companies are in the *striving for clarity*. We took time to explain things in a way that helps people understand what, when, and why it matters. Take the "what do we need to tell people" and layer over the "what do we *want* to tell people" and "what's the best way of articulating it."

`20:36` **Matt:** While most organizations are in this clarity area, the question I would always ask is: who's writing the communication? Who's reviewing the communication? Who's approving the communication? Because **our mutual friend Steve Crescenzo** also often talks about *writing for the approval process*. We write in a way that's just going to get everyone to nod and go "Yeah, send that thing out" — knowing that when it lands, everyone goes, "What is this? I don't understand it."

`21:12` **Matt:** Part of our role as communicators is to look at communication from the audience's perspective and go, "Right, okay. You may understand it because you're a benefits person, but me — in the factory, on the shop floor, in the office, busy parent — am I going to understand what this means?" The more testing, refinement, involvement we can have with real people, the better.

`21:42` **Tarek:** Let me ask you a question. **Why do you think we are in this state?** When I think about marketing, for example, they can't just say "well, we put up some ads" — maybe they can, but they shouldn't. Of course, you need to measure, optimize. What's your ROI? Is it connecting with our intended audience? What's the return on our investment in this platform? We report on all of that. **How did we get to a state where something so important as employee benefits is reduced to checking a box?**

`22:25` **Matt:** That is a jolly good question. I would say it's probably down to prioritization and time. I don't want to trivialize it — we could talk more about it — but doing this well takes time, takes investment, takes headspace. We're chasing the £10,000 or $30,000 of investment, where we've got to dedicate good time to keep benefits on the radar in front of people.

`22:55` **Matt:** You know that stat, like, someone needs to see something **seven times** before it sinks in? Lots of things in the real world where people go, "Yeah, unless it's interesting, I'm not going to give it any attention." But then when it comes to benefit communication, it's like, "Well, we'll just cover the details, and if someone wants it, they'll go and find it." I kind of feel like that's table stakes.

`23:20` **Matt:** If an organization from a values perspective is saying "you're our greatest asset" — those two things are kind of juxtaposed. "Our greatest asset" — and also, for most companies, our single greatest line item in the budget is people.

`23:42` **Tarek:** For most companies, at least in the US, **70% of the budget is people**. Marketing is a sliver of that, but they full circle. Is it working? Refining, whatever. And we're spending so much on our people. It has to be time and competing priorities. It's a big number.

`24:04` **Matt:** Yeah. But again — we'll talk about this towards the end of how we actually measure the impact of all of this work. From a US benefits strategy perspective, it's like: how do we control the big-ticket items, which are obviously healthcare spend? In the UK, some of the big-ticket items in the recent past have been pensions and retirement-based saving vehicles. So it's "how do we keep these costs under control?" — and we're managing the top bit, not necessarily how people *feel* about it. Are people using it? Are people feeling good about it? Some of those things aren't always squared away.

`24:46` **Matt:** **Stage 3 — Relevance.** Let me quickly walk through. Compliance is bare-minimum, table-stakes stuff. Clarity — are we actually spending the time to explain in a way that people understand? The relevance piece — we see more people starting to dabble in this area: take the whole story and messaging and **carve it up in a way that's relevant to different groups.** We've talked about personas and segmentation — that would be its own webinar.

`25:19` **Matt:** As opposed to *everything to everyone in the same way*, how do we organize content in different ways to different people at different times? So when it lands with me, it goes "okay, it knows that I'm a 40-something-year-old, married, two kids" — because the data exists. It knows that about me.

`25:51` **Matt:** **Stage 4 — Connection.** How can technology, how can data, how can we be smart on showing up in those important moments? The connection piece is the bigger bit for me. As I say, ties into organization values and employee value proposition, which we'll touch on a bit later — but **how do we connect benefits to the bigger piece?** Benefits, reward, HR — the whole thing. "Come to work, this is a really important part." How do we make sure that connection is there?
