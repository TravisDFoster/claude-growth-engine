# Building a Case for Comms Investment Using Data

Most internal communicators know when their program is working. Open rates are up. Employees are actually reading the benefits update. The CEO town hall recap got more clicks than anything sent last quarter. The problem is that none of that means anything to a CFO who is looking at a budget line and deciding whether to fund a headcount request or a new platform.

The gap is not about the data. The data is usually there. The gap is in translation — taking what comms teams measure and connecting it to outcomes that move budget conversations forward. This post walks through how to do that.

## What Metrics Leadership Actually Cares About

Leadership is not indifferent to internal communications data. They are indifferent to the *wrong* communications data presented without context.

Open rates and send volumes are useful internally, but they do not map cleanly to anything a VP of Finance or a Chief People Officer uses to evaluate investment decisions. To make the case, you need to reframe what you are already tracking into the categories leadership thinks in: risk, productivity, and retention.

**Risk and compliance** is often the fastest path to executive attention. When employees miss a critical policy update, a safety communication, or a benefits deadline, the organization absorbs the cost — whether that shows up as HR support tickets, regulatory exposure, or operational errors. Read receipts and acknowledgment data are not just comms metrics. They are documentation that the organization met its communication obligation. Framing them this way changes the conversation.

**Productivity and time** is a less obvious angle but often a persuasive one. When a message is unclear or fails to reach the intended audience, someone has to clean it up — a manager answering repeat questions, an HR coordinator fielding calls that a targeted re-send could have prevented, a team lead running a meeting that existed only because the original communication did not land. These costs are real and estimable even if they are not tracked precisely.

**Retention and culture** is the third category, and while it carries more nuance, the underlying data is strong. Gallup research consistently shows that employees who feel well-informed by their organization are more engaged, and disengaged employees cost organizations an estimated 34% of their annual salary in lost productivity. Communicators who can connect program gaps to disengagement risk are speaking a language the C-suite already understands.

One concrete benchmark worth citing in any executive conversation: St. Elizabeth Healthcare, a Cerkl Broadcast customer, achieved a 54% employee open rate on internal communications. That number matters not because it is impressive in isolation, but because it shows that high reach is achievable with the right tools and targeting — and that industry-standard email open rates of 20–25% represent a meaningful gap in how many employees are actually receiving critical information.

## How to Frame Engagement Data as Business Impact

The most common mistake communicators make when presenting data to leadership is leading with a dashboard instead of a narrative. A metrics report without a story attached is asking leadership to do work you should have done for them.

The framework that works consistently is simple: start with the metric, connect it to a business outcome, then quantify or estimate the impact. You do not need precise numbers. A well-constructed directional argument is often more persuasive than a complex model with weak assumptions.

Here is what that looks like in practice. Instead of reporting that open rate dropped 18% among warehouse staff during the benefits enrollment window, you frame it this way: "Our open rate dropped 18% among warehouse staff during benefits enrollment. That likely contributed to the 12% increase in HR support tickets we saw that month, and our estimate is that it cost approximately 40 hours of HR staff time to resolve questions that a targeted re-send could have addressed in one send." That framing gives leadership something to evaluate. The dashboard version gives them a number that does not connect to anything they own.

Three techniques make this translation work consistently. A before/after comparison shows movement over time, especially useful when a new tool or process was introduced — it creates a causal story even without controlled conditions. A cost-of-inaction argument estimates what happens without investment: the HR overhead, the turnover risk, the compliance gaps that go unresolved. Benchmarking provides context by comparing your internal rates to industry standards, which is the difference between "our open rate is 31%" and "our open rate is 31%, compared to an industry benchmark of 54% for organizations using purpose-built internal comms platforms."

## A Step-by-Step Structure for the Comms ROI Conversation

The following five steps give you a repeatable structure for any budget conversation, whether you are asking for a new tool, additional headcount, or protected time to build out your measurement practice.

**Step 1: Know the specific ask before you walk in.** Vague requests do not survive committee. Be precise about what you need: a specific tool license, a defined headcount number, additional seat access, or a budget line for a campaign. Specificity signals that you have thought through the investment, not just identified a problem.

**Step 2: Anchor to a problem leadership already owns.** Do not open with "comms needs better tools." Open with a business problem that is already on someone's radar, then connect it to a communications gap. "We had three missed compliance deadlines last quarter because messages were not reaching field teams. Here is what that cost us" is a different entry point than a capability pitch, and it is far more likely to get a second meeting.

**Step 3: Show the current state with data you already have.** Pull send volume, open and click rates, audience coverage by segment, HR ticket volume, or survey feedback. Even rough data tells a story if you frame it honestly. Communicators often underestimate how much they already know — the hesitation is usually about confidence in interpretation, not absence of information.

**Step 4: Model what better looks like.** Describe the improved state and what it would take to reach it. Use conservative estimates and be transparent about your assumptions. Overclaiming erodes credibility faster than any gap in the data set.

**Step 5: Frame the ask as ROI, not cost.** "This platform pays for itself if it prevents two compliance incidents per quarter" lands differently than "the annual license is $X." Leadership is accustomed to evaluating investments against returns. Help them do that math rather than asking them to evaluate a line item in isolation.

## The Shift That Makes the Conversation Work

Comms investment conversations go better when communicators stop defending activity and start quantifying outcomes. The data to do this is almost always already available — it just needs to be reframed in terms leadership actually uses to make decisions.

That shift requires a different relationship with your own analytics. Instead of reporting what you sent and who opened it, start asking what your data implies about risk, time, and cost. Once you can answer those questions, even directionally, you are no longer asking for a budget. You are making a business argument.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is internal communications ROI and how is it measured?**

Internal communications ROI is the return an organization gets from investing in its employee communication programs relative to what it spends on tools, staff, and time. It is measured by connecting communication outcomes — like open rates, audience reach, acknowledgment rates, and send volume — to business outcomes like compliance rates, HR ticket volume, employee retention, and productivity. The most practical measurement approach connects specific communication events to downstream business indicators rather than trying to calculate a single aggregate return figure.

**What data should I use to build a comms investment business case?**

Start with data you already have: send volume, open and click rates, audience reach by segment, acknowledgment or read-receipt data, and any survey feedback on communication quality. Pair those metrics with downstream indicators like HR support ticket volume, compliance incident rates, or turnover trends to build a narrative that connects communication performance to business outcomes. You do not need perfect data — a directional argument with honest assumptions is often enough to move the conversation forward.

**How do I measure internal communications effectiveness?**

Effectiveness measurement starts with defining what "working" means for a given communication. For a compliance update, effectiveness might be acknowledgment rate and HR ticket volume. For a culture initiative, it might be survey response rates and engagement scores. For an operational update, it might be whether the information reached frontline employees before a shift change. Map each send type to a relevant outcome metric rather than applying a single universal standard across all content types.

**What metrics do executives care about in internal comms?**

Executives respond most consistently to metrics that connect to risk (did employees receive and acknowledge compliance-critical information?), productivity (how much staff time is being spent resolving communication gaps?), and retention (what does poor employee communication cost in disengagement or turnover?). Open rates and send volume become meaningful when they are translated into these business-adjacent terms rather than presented as standalone comms metrics.

**How do I justify the cost of internal communications software?**

Frame the software investment against the cost of the status quo — the HR overhead generated by missed communications, the risk exposure from messages that do not reach field or frontline workers, and the staff time spent manually managing sends, lists, and follow-ups. A specific, conservative estimate of avoided costs is more persuasive than a features comparison. For example: if a purpose-built platform prevents one compliance incident per quarter that would otherwise generate 40 hours of HR and legal staff time, the math on most platform costs becomes straightforward.
