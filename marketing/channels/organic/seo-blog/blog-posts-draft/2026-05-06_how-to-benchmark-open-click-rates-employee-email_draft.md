## How to Benchmark Open and Click Rates for Employee Email

Most internal communicators know their open rate. Far fewer know whether it's actually good. The problem isn't measuring — it's that internal email operates in a different context than any external benchmark most people have access to. Marketing email norms don't apply here, and treating them as a reference point will consistently mislead you. This post covers what real employee email open and click rates look like, what the gap between those two numbers tells you, and how segmentation shifts the picture.

## What "Good" Looks Like for Internal Email

The most important thing to know about employee email open rate benchmarks is that they run significantly higher than marketing email benchmarks. Consumer-facing email campaigns typically aim for open rates in the 20–30% range. Internal email operates in a different environment entirely.

A reasonable target range for employee email open rates is 40–60%. Well-segmented sends from a trusted sender at the right time can reach 70% or higher. For click rates, 10–25% is a healthy range — though that ceiling depends heavily on whether the email includes a clear action for the recipient to take. Informational updates that don't ask employees to do anything will naturally cluster at the lower end. Sends requiring enrollment, RSVPs, or acknowledgment should be targeting the upper half of that range.

Cerkl customers bear this out. St. Elizabeth Healthcare sees a 54% open rate on employee email and a 46% open rate on medical-staff sends. Stanislaus State reaches a 37% open rate on staff digests. Paycor has achieved a 34% click rate. These aren't cherry-picked numbers to make the platform look good — they reflect what's achievable when email is built for an internal audience with proper segmentation and consistent sending cadence.

### Why Internal Benchmarks Are Higher Than Marketing Benchmarks

The fundamental difference is the nature of the relationship. Employees have a structural reason to open internal email that has nothing to do with personal interest or curiosity. The email comes from their organization, often from HR, leadership, or a team they work with directly. That creates a baseline level of engagement that marketing email can't assume. Employees also don't self-select onto internal email lists the way consumers opt into brand communications — which means the audience is already defined and relatively stable.

That said, a captive audience is not the same as an engaged one. Internal email that falls below the 40% open rate threshold isn't a minor underperformance. It's a meaningful signal that something is wrong — timing, subject lines, send frequency, sender trust, or relevance to the audience receiving it.

## Why the Open-to-Click Gap Matters

Open rate and click rate are related but distinct measurements. Open rate tells you how many recipients viewed the email. Click rate tells you how many took an action within it. Both matter, but the relationship between them often tells you more than either metric on its own.

A healthy gap looks different depending on what you sent. An informational newsletter with company news, leadership updates, and project highlights might pull a 55% open rate and a 12% click rate — that's expected. Employees read the update and moved on. No action was required. The gap is wide, but it's not a problem.

The gap becomes a warning when action was required. If you sent a benefits enrollment reminder with a 58% open rate and a 6% click rate, that's a meaningful failure. More than half your employees opened the email and didn't complete the action you needed them to complete. The same logic applies to policy acknowledgments, event RSVPs, safety training sign-ups, and survey links. When an email is designed to drive a specific behavior and the click rate doesn't follow the open rate, the email's content, CTA design, or perceived urgency needs to be examined.

The other warning pattern is low opens paired with low clicks. That's almost always an upstream problem — subject line, sender name, send time, or a deliverability issue — rather than a content problem. If people aren't opening, you can't draw conclusions about click behavior at all.

The practical approach is to classify your sends by type before looking at performance data. Treat informational sends and action-required sends as separate categories with different benchmarks. Averaging across both types will obscure what's actually happening.

## Why Segmentation Changes Everything

The most predictable way to improve internal email benchmarks is to send more targeted emails. Segmentation matters in internal comms for the same reason it matters anywhere else: relevance drives opens, and relevance drives clicks. An email that feels written for the person receiving it will outperform a company-wide blast on almost every measurable dimension.

The segmentation levers available to most internal comms teams include role or department, location or region, employment status (full-time, part-time, contractor), tenure, and workforce type (office, remote, frontline). A benefits update sent only to benefits-eligible employees will perform differently — better — than the same update sent organization-wide. A safety protocol email for field workers is more likely to be opened by field workers who receive it alone than by field workers who receive it alongside content that has nothing to do with their work.

Benchmarking segmented sends requires a slightly different frame. The goal isn't to compare a targeted segment against an industry average — it's to compare the segment against your own historical sends and against each other. A 45% open rate on a tightly targeted 80-person segment may represent stronger performance than a 60% open rate on a company-wide blast of 3,000, especially if the targeted send drove a higher proportion of the action you were looking for. Internal communications email benchmarks are most useful when they're calibrated to your organization's own history, not treated as a fixed external standard.

Poor segmentation is one of the most consistent reasons internal email metrics plateau. When everyone gets everything, relevance drops, unsubscribes or passive disengagement accumulate, and open rates gradually compress toward a mediocre average. Segmentation is one of the clearest levers for reversing that trajectory.

## Turning Benchmarks Into Action

Benchmarks give you context, not conclusions. The number matters less than whether it's improving and what's driving the gap. Three steps make benchmark data actionable.

First, establish your baseline. If you're not currently tracking open and click rates at the send level, that's the starting point. You can't benchmark what you haven't measured, and aggregate averages calculated after the fact have limited diagnostic value. You need per-send data that you can segment by audience and send type.

Second, break down results before drawing conclusions. A single average open rate across all sends can be misleading when it combines a 70% open rate on a CEO all-hands email with a 28% open rate on an automated announcement. Separate your data by send type, department or audience segment, and time period before deciding what the numbers mean.

Third, identify your largest gap. If open rate is the problem, start with subject lines, sender name, and send timing. If open rate is strong but click rate is weak on action-required sends, look at the CTA itself — its placement, the language, and whether the ask is clear. Each gap points to a different fix, and working on the wrong variable wastes time.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a good open rate for employee email?

A healthy open rate for internal employee email falls between 40% and 60%. High-performing sends that are well-segmented, timely, and come from a trusted sender can reach 70% or higher. These ranges are significantly above consumer marketing email benchmarks, which typically target 20–30%, because employees have a structural relationship with internal communications that external audiences do not.

### What is a good click rate for internal email?

A click rate between 10% and 25% is a reasonable target range for internal email, though the right expectation depends on the type of send. Informational updates that don't ask employees to take action will naturally see lower click rates. Emails requiring enrollment, survey responses, event RSVPs, or policy acknowledgments should target the higher end of the range — a wide gap between opens and clicks on an action-required send is a signal worth investigating.

### How are internal email benchmarks different from marketing email benchmarks?

Internal email benchmarks are significantly higher than external marketing benchmarks because the audience and context are different. Employees receive internal communications from their own organization, which creates a baseline engagement level that consumer brand email cannot assume. Employees also don't self-select onto internal email lists the way marketing subscribers do. That said, a captive audience is not automatically an engaged one — low internal benchmarks still indicate a real problem with relevance, timing, or content.

### How does segmentation affect internal email open and click rates?

Segmented sends consistently outperform broad company-wide blasts on open rate and click rate because relevance drives engagement. When employees receive content that is directly relevant to their role, location, tenure, or employment status, they are more likely to open it and act on it. Poor segmentation is one of the most common reasons internal email metrics plateau over time. Benchmarking segmented sends against your own historical averages — not against a static industry number — gives you a more accurate read on whether performance is improving.

### How do I start benchmarking my internal email performance?

Start by establishing a baseline with per-send open and click data. If you don't currently have that visibility, getting it is step one. Then separate your sends by type (informational vs. action-required) and by audience segment before drawing conclusions. Identify where the largest gap is — between open rate and your benchmark, or between open rate and click rate on sends that need action — and work on the variable that's most responsible for the gap.
