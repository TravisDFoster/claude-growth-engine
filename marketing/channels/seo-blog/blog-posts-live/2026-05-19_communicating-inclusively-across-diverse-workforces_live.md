# Communicating Inclusively Across Diverse Workforces

Most internal comms teams say they want to communicate inclusively. Then they send the same email, in the same format, through the same channel, to every employee. AAPI Heritage Month is a useful test case. If your only move is adding a banner to the intranet homepage in May, you are checking a box. The harder question is what your current infrastructure allows you to do, and who it leaves out by default.

## What Inclusive Internal Communications Actually Means

The conversation about inclusive internal communications tends to get stuck on language. Teams focus on word choice, terminology reviews, and style guides. Those things matter, but they are not the bottleneck. The bottleneck is structural.

Most organizations run their employee communications through a single channel, in a single format, on a single cadence. That architecture excludes by design, even when the content itself is thoughtful and well-intentioned. Language access is the clearest example: employees whose first language is not English, or who work in roles with low document exposure, are often reading communications written at a reading level or in a register that does not match how they receive information. Channel access compounds this. Roughly 80 percent of the global workforce is deskless, according to commonly cited research from Emergence Capital and Workleap. Frontline workers, field teams, and manufacturing employees frequently do not have a corporate email address or regular intranet access, which means the communication never reaches them at all regardless of how carefully it was written.

Beyond language and channel, there is a dimension that gets less attention: cultural relevance. Heritage months, ERG initiatives, and community acknowledgments only land as genuine if they name specific groups with specificity, arrive through the right channels, and reflect input from the people being recognized. Generic "celebrating diversity" language sent org-wide does not accomplish any of that. Timing and format matter too. A shift worker in a distribution center is unlikely to engage with a 600-word all-staff email that arrives at 9 a.m. on a Tuesday. The format and cadence of communication is itself an inclusion decision.

## How Segmentation Makes Inclusive Comms Possible

Inclusive intent does not scale without segmentation. This is the technical foundation that most teams are missing. Segmentation converts a good-faith goal into a communications practice that reaches people differently based on who they are, where they work, and how they access information.

What segmentation makes possible in practice is meaningful. A team communicating with a multilingual workforce can route language-appropriate content to employee populations by region, primary language, or role type, rather than publishing one version and hoping it works for everyone. Deskless and frontline workers can receive mobile-friendly content or SMS-accessible messages instead of emails that will never land in an inbox they check. Heritage month acknowledgments can be targeted to employees who have opted in or identified as part of a specific community, rather than blasted across the entire organization where they risk reading as performative to employees outside that community. Cadence and format can be adjusted by function. Field teams often engage better with shorter, higher-frequency messages, while headquarters employees may want richer context and longer reads.

## The AAPI Heritage Month Example: What Doing It Right Looks Like

May is AAPI Heritage Month in the United States, recognized by Congress since 1992. It is a useful test case because so many internal comms teams handle it in ways that illustrate what inclusive communications is not.

What most teams do: a graphic goes up on the intranet, maybe an all-staff email goes out with a subject line like "Celebrating Our AAPI Colleagues." The intent is genuine. The execution sends the message to everyone, gets written without meaningful input from AAPI employees, and ends there. There is no follow-up, no measurement, and no signal that the team understood who they were trying to reach or why.

What inclusive comms teams do is different at every stage. They partner with AAPI Employee Resource Groups or employee councils to shape the message, not as a review step but as a co-creation process. Content about employees should not be created without them. The acknowledgment reaches employees who have opted in or identified with the AAPI community, delivered through the channels those employees use, rather than going org-wide in a format that can feel like a broadcast to a group you are not part of. The month also serves as a listening moment: a pulse check with AAPI employees on belonging and communication effectiveness, not a campaign you send and then close out.

The AAPI example is not the point in isolation. The point is that it generalizes. Any heritage month, ERG-led initiative, or cultural moment requires the same infrastructure to land as genuine rather than performative: segmentation, co-creation, channel access, and a feedback loop. Without those things, good intentions produce communications that reach the wrong people at the wrong time through the wrong format.

## Measuring Whether Inclusive Comms Is Working

Inclusive communication that goes unmeasured cannot improve. If you cannot tell whether your AAPI Heritage Month content reached AAPI employees, you do not know whether it was inclusive. You only know that it went out.

The metrics worth tracking are not complicated, but they require segmentation data to be meaningful. Reach by segment tells you whether you are getting through to the populations you are targeting. Engagement rate by demographic or role group surfaces which segments are systematically underperforming. A consistent gap in open or click rates within a specific employee population is a signal worth investigating. Opt-in and opt-out patterns reveal whether certain groups are disengaging from your communications channel over time. Survey or pulse feedback tied to belonging and communication effectiveness closes the qualitative loop that quantitative data alone cannot close.

This is where the infrastructure connects back to the outcome. Segmentation data and unified analytics allow inclusive intent to produce inclusive impact, and they allow you to show stakeholders that your communications are reaching the people who need to receive them, not going out into the void. That is a measurable communications effectiveness argument, not only an equity argument. Inclusive communications is both.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is inclusive internal communications?

Inclusive internal communications is the practice of designing employee communication systems so that every employee, regardless of language, role, location, or how they access information, receives relevant content through channels they can use. It goes beyond word choice and tone to address the structural elements of how communication is built and distributed: channel access, segmentation, timing, format, and feedback loops.

### Why does channel access matter for inclusive communications?

Roughly 80 percent of the global workforce is deskless, meaning frontline workers, field employees, and manufacturing staff who often lack regular access to corporate email or company intranets. If your communication infrastructure relies exclusively on email or intranet, it systematically excludes these populations regardless of how inclusive the content itself is. Reaching these employees requires routing communications through mobile-friendly or SMS-accessible channels.

### How does segmentation support inclusive communication?

Segmentation lets you send different versions of communications to different employee populations based on factors like role, location, primary language, or ERG membership. Without segmentation, inclusive intent cannot scale. You send one message in one format to everyone and hope it works. With segmentation, you can route language-appropriate content to multilingual employees, deliver heritage month acknowledgments to employees who have opted in to receive them, and adjust cadence and format for different work patterns.

### How should an internal comms team approach AAPI Heritage Month?

Start by partnering with your AAPI Employee Resource Group or employee council to shape and co-create the content. Do not write about employees without them. Use your audience segments to reach employees who have opted in or identified as part of the AAPI community rather than sending org-wide. Treat the month as a listening opportunity and use it to pulse check with AAPI employees on belonging and communication effectiveness. Measure engagement within that specific segment to understand resonance, not just overall open rates.

### What metrics help measure inclusive communications effectiveness?

The most useful metrics are segment-specific: reach by employee segment (are you getting through to the populations you're targeting?), engagement rate by demographic or role group (are some groups consistently underperforming?), opt-in and opt-out patterns over time (are certain employee groups disengaging?), and survey or pulse results tied to belonging and communication effectiveness. Generic org-wide open rates will not surface inclusion gaps. You need segment-level data to see what is working.
