### Section 1: Why Email Clients Fall Short for Measurement (150–175 words)

- Gmail and Outlook show you: sent, delivered, bounced — and that is roughly it
- No native open tracking at scale, no click attribution, no audience-level breakdowns, no historical send performance, no way to compare campaigns or segments over time
- Distribution list management compounds the problem: lists are static, manual to maintain, and give you no behavioral data
- The result: internal comms teams cannot answer basic questions leadership increasingly expects them to answer — "Did employees actually read that?" "Which locations have the lowest engagement?" "Is our all-staff newsletter working?"
- Transition: purpose-built internal comms tools track what email clients ignore

---

### Section 2: What Analytics Features Actually Matter (200–225 words)

Frame this as what to look for — practical, not a feature dump. Tie each point to a communicator's real need.

- **Open rate by send**: The baseline signal. What percentage of recipients opened the message? More useful when trended over time, not just per blast.
- **Click-through rate**: Did employees take action? Especially important for high-stakes sends (enrollment deadlines, policy acknowledgments, event registration).
- **Audience reach**: Not just opens — how many unique employees were touched? Useful for proving reach across a distributed workforce.
- **Segment-level performance**: Breakdown by department, location, role, or any dynamic audience group. This is where insight gets strategic — you can identify which groups are consistently underperforming and adjust.
- **Send history and trend lines**: Single-blast data is noise. Patterns across sends are signal. Good analytics surfaces improvement or drop-off over time.
- **Unsubscribes and opt-outs**: A health metric, not just a compliance flag. Spikes tell you something is off with frequency or relevance.
