### Core Argument (One Sentence)
Inclusive internal communication is not about using the right words — it is about building the infrastructure to reach every employee with content that is relevant to who they are, where they work, and what they need.

---

### Outline

**I. Opening Hook (50–80 words)**
- Lead with a concrete tension: most internal comms teams say they want inclusive communication, but they send the same message in the same format through the same channel to everyone.
- Tie to May: AAPI Heritage Month is a good test case. If your only move is adding a banner to your intranet, you are not communicating inclusively — you are checking a box.
- Establish the real question: what does it actually take to communicate in a way that reaches and resonates with a genuinely diverse workforce?

**II. What Inclusive Internal Comms Actually Means in Practice (150–200 words)**
- Move past the "tone and terminology" framing that dominates most inclusive comms content. That matters, but it is not the bottleneck.
- The real gap is structural: most teams have one channel, one format, one cadence, and no segmentation. That architecture excludes by default.
- Key dimensions of real inclusivity in internal comms:
  - Language and reading level (employees whose first language is not English; roles with low document exposure)
  - Channel access (deskless workers, frontline staff, and field employees often do not have corporate email or intranet access)
  - Cultural relevance (heritage months, community acknowledgments, and milestone recognition that name specific groups — not generic "celebrating diversity" language)
  - Timing and format (shift workers may miss time-sensitive messages; long-form prose excludes employees who read in short windows)

**III. How Segmentation Makes Inclusive Comms Possible (200–250 words)**
- Segmentation is the technical foundation for inclusive communication. Without it, inclusive intent cannot scale.
- What segmentation enables:
  - Sending language-appropriate content to employee segments by region, primary language, or role type
  - Routing deskless and frontline workers to mobile-friendly or SMS-accessible content instead of assuming inbox access
  - Targeting culturally relevant acknowledgments to specific employee communities (e.g., AAPI Heritage Month content delivered to employees who have indicated interest or belong to relevant ERGs) rather than blasting org-wide
  - Adjusting cadence and format by function — field teams may need shorter, higher-frequency messages; headquarters employees may want deeper context
- Mention Cerkl's dynamic segmentation: HRIS-synced, live segments that update automatically when employee data changes — no manual list management, no IT dependency for every send
- Key point: you cannot segment what you have not synced. Employee data accuracy is the prerequisite.

**IV. The AAPI Heritage Month Example — Doing It Right (150–175 words)**
- May is AAPI Heritage Month. Use this as a specific, practical case for how inclusive comms differs from performative comms.
- What most teams do: post a graphic on the intranet, maybe send an all-staff email with a generic "celebrating our AAPI colleagues" message.
- What inclusive comms teams do differently:
  - Partner with AAPI ERG or employee resource council to shape the message and spotlight — do not create content about employees without them
  - Segment acknowledgment content so it reaches employees who have opted in or identified as part of the AAPI community, rather than blasting a message that lands as performative to those outside the community
  - Use the month as a listening moment — pulse check with AAPI employees on belonging and inclusion, not just a send-and-forget campaign
  - Measure engagement from the targeted segment to understand resonance, not just open rate across the org
- The AAPI example generalizes: any heritage month, ERG-led initiative, or cultural moment requires the same infrastructure.

**V. Measuring Whether Inclusive Comms Is Working (100–130 words)**
- Inclusive communication that goes unmeasured cannot improve.
- Metrics to watch:
  - Reach by segment — are you actually getting through to the populations you are targeting?
  - Engagement rate by demographic or role group — are some segments systematically underperforming? That is a signal.
  - Opt-in and opt-out patterns — are certain employee groups disengaging from comms over time?
  - Survey or pulse feedback tied to belonging and communication effectiveness
- Note: this is where segmentation data and analytics close the loop. If you cannot measure by segment, you cannot know whether your inclusive intent translated into inclusive impact.
- Connects to the campaign theme: Prove Your Impact. Inclusive comms is not just an equity goal — it is a measurable communications effectiveness goal.

### Supporting Details and Research Hooks

- AAPI Heritage Month: May is the official AAPI Heritage Month in the US. Acknowledged by Congress since 1992. Relevant ERG structures, executive acknowledgment, and heritage programming are common in mid-market and enterprise organizations.
- Workforce diversity stat hook: US workforce is increasingly diverse — look for Pew, BLS, or McKinsey data on workforce demographics to open or support the post.
