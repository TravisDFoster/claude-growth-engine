## Outline for Writing

### Working Thesis

Internal communicators already have engagement data — open rates, click-throughs, segment breakdowns — but most don't have a clear system for translating those numbers into better messages. This post gives them a practical, three-part framework: use open rates to sharpen subject lines, use click data to tighten CTAs, and use segment performance to guide targeting. The goal is to help communicators move from passive reporting to active improvement.

---

### Intro (75–100 words)

Open with the tension: communicators are under more pressure than ever to prove their impact, and most already have access to analytics — but having data and knowing what to do with it are two different things. Many teams look at open rates and click percentages after a send and then move on to the next blast without changing anything. This post is about closing that loop. A quick preview of the three signals covered: open rates, click data, and segment performance.

---

### Section 1: Open Rates Tell You Whether Your Subject Line Is Working (175–225 words)

**Core message:** Open rate is a direct reflection of whether your subject line and send timing earned attention. It is the one metric you can use to improve before the reader ever sees your content.

**What to cover:**
- What a "good" internal email open rate looks like — the 2026 Insights Update benchmark range to anchor expectations (reference Cerkl benchmark data where available; internal comms open rates typically run significantly higher than external marketing emails)
- How to read a low open rate: subject line too vague, too long, too corporate, too frequent, or wrong send time
- A practical test: compare open rates across your last five to ten sends. What did the higher-performing ones have in common? Specificity? Urgency? A person's name or department in the subject line?
- One actionable move: identify your two lowest-performing sends from the last quarter and rewrite their subject lines using what you learned

**Tone note:** Be specific and give communicators a real starting point — not just "test your subject lines" but how to evaluate what you already have.

---

### Section 2: Click Data Tells You Whether Your CTA Is Doing Its Job (175–225 words)

**Core message:** Click-through rate shows whether the content inside the email motivated action. Low clicks usually mean the CTA is buried, vague, or misaligned with what the reader actually cares about.

**What to cover:**
- The difference between open rate (did they open?) and click rate (did they do anything?): a high open rate with low clicks means the content or CTA is the problem, not the subject line
- Common CTA mistakes in internal comms: too many links competing for attention, CTAs written as passive invitations ("click here for more info") versus direct asks ("submit your benefits selection by Friday")
- What to look at: which links in a multi-link email got clicked versus which were ignored — this tells you what content your audience actually cares about
- One actionable move: in your next send, limit the email to one primary CTA and compare click rate to your average — this is a fast and low-risk test

**Supporting detail:** Reference that Foundations Insights shows link-level click tracking so communicators can see exactly which content drove action — not just total clicks.

---

### Section 3: Segment Performance Tells You Whether You Are Reaching the Right People (175–200 words)

**Core message:** Aggregate engagement numbers can hide what is really happening. Segment-level data — by department, location, role, or employment type — shows you whether certain groups are consistently tuning out, which means the content may not be relevant to them.

**What to cover:**
- Why aggregate open rates can be misleading: a 45% open rate across the company might mean 70% of corporate employees opened and 20% of field workers opened — and those two audiences need very different messages
- What segment data reveals: content relevance gaps, targeting mismatches, or audiences who need a different channel or format entirely
- Practical use case: if operations employees consistently under-engage with HR benefits emails, it may mean the content is written for desk workers and the language needs to shift — or the send should be segmented so each group gets a version written for their reality
- One actionable move: pull your last three sends and compare open and click rates by the segments you have available — look for consistent outliers, high or low

### Closing / Transition to CTA (75–100 words)

Reframe the takeaway: engagement data is not a report card — it is a feedback loop. Every send is an opportunity to learn something about your audience, and the communicators who improve over time are the ones who actually use that signal. Close with a forward-looking statement: these three metrics are a starting point. As teams grow and communication complexity increases, more advanced analytics and segmentation capabilities become available — but the habit of acting on data starts here. ### Notes for Writing

- **Tone:** Practical and direct. This audience is not new to comms — they are experienced communicators who feel stuck because they have data but no system for using it. Avoid being condescending; treat them as professionals who just need a clearer framework.
- **Avoid:** Generic statements like "data is important" or "analytics can help your team." Every sentence should give the reader something specific to do or think about.
- **Cerkl product mentions:** Keep light and natural. The Insights dashboard and segment-level data are relevant and should be mentioned — but this post should read as genuinely helpful to any internal communicator, not as a product walkthrough.
- **Benchmarks:** If referencing open rate benchmarks, tie to the 2026 Insights Update campaign framing. Avoid citing outdated stats.
- **Internal link opportunities:** Consider linking to any Cerkl benchmark or analytics-related content (e.g., internal comms benchmarks report, open rate benchmark posts) and potentially to the Foundations free signup or Insights feature page.
