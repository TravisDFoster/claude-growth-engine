## Outline for Writing

### Introduction (50–75 words)
- Open with the core tension: most internal communicators send emails but have no external reference point for whether their numbers are good or not.
- Acknowledge that benchmarking internal email is different from marketing email — different audiences, different goals, different norms.

---

### Section 1: What Are Typical Open and Click Rates for Employee Email? (150–200 words)

**H2: What "Good" Looks Like for Internal Email**

- Internal email benchmarks are significantly higher than external/marketing email. This is important context — don't judge internal results against consumer email standards.
- Benchmark ranges to use:
  - Open rate: 40–60% is a reasonable target range for employee email. High-performing sends (well-segmented, timely, from a trusted sender) can reach 70%+.
  - Click rate: 10–25% is a healthy range. Click rates depend heavily on whether the email has a compelling call to action.
- Reference real-world context: Cerkl customer examples include St. Elizabeth Healthcare at 54% open rate, Stanislaus State at 37% staff open rate.
- Note: benchmarks vary by industry, workforce type, and how email is structured (newsletter digest vs. urgent blast).

**Why Internal Benchmarks Are Higher Than Marketing Benchmarks**
- Employees have a captive, relationship-based reason to open — they are the audience, not the prospect.
- Opt-in dynamics are different: employees don't choose to be on the list the same way consumers do.
- However, this also means disengagement is harder to diagnose — low numbers signal a real problem.

---

### Section 2: How to Read the Gap Between Open Rate and Click Rate (200–250 words)

**H2: Why the Open-to-Click Gap Matters**

- Define the two metrics clearly for readers who may be newer to analytics:
  - Open rate = percentage of recipients who opened the email
  - Click rate = percentage of recipients who clicked a link in the email
- Explain what a healthy gap looks like vs. a warning-sign gap:
  - Healthy: High opens, moderate clicks — employees are reading but not every email needs an action
  - Warning: High opens, very low clicks — content isn't driving action when it should (survey links, policy acknowledgments, event RSVPs going untouched)
  - Warning: Low opens, low clicks — delivery, timing, or subject line problem upstream of engagement
- Introduce the concept of "send type" as a factor:
  - Informational sends (news, updates): high opens, low clicks are expected and fine
  - Action-required sends (policy signoffs, enrollment, events): high clicks should be the target — a big gap here signals a problem
- Practical takeaway: look at open-to-click gap per send type, not as a single average across all emails.

---

### Section 3: How Segmentation Affects Performance (150–200 words)

**H2: Why Segmentation Changes Everything**

- Segmented emails consistently outperform broad-list sends. This is true in internal comms just as it is in marketing email.
- Explain why: relevance drives opens and clicks. An email that feels written for someone will outperform a company-wide blast on almost every metric.
- Practical segmentation examples for internal comms:
  - Role or department (e.g., a benefits update only sent to benefits-eligible employees)
  - Location or region (relevant for multi-site or remote/hybrid workforces)
  - Tenure or employment status (onboarding vs. long-tenured employees)
  - Frontline vs. office workers (different channels, different content needs)
- How to benchmark segmented sends: compare segment performance to each other and against your own historical average, not just to a static industry benchmark. A 45% open rate for a targeted segment may outperform a 60% open rate on a company-wide blast if the segment drove more action.
- Note: poor segmentation is one of the most common reasons internal email metrics plateau — and fixing it is one of the clearest levers for improvement.

---

### Section 4: What to Do With Your Numbers (100–150 words)

**H2: Turning Benchmarks Into Action**

- Benchmarks are a starting point, not a verdict. The goal is trend improvement over time, not hitting a specific number.
- Three practical steps:
  1. Establish your baseline — if you don't have analytics, this is step one. You can't benchmark what you haven't measured.
  2. Segment your analysis — break down results by send type, department, or audience before drawing conclusions.
  3. Identify your biggest gap — is it open rate, click rate, or both? Each signals a different fix (subject lines and send timing vs. content relevance and CTAs).
