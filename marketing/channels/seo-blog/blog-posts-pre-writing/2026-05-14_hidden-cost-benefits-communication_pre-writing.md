### Introduction (~150 words)

- Open with the visible cost: $28,250 per employee per year (BLS) on benefits in the US, £10,000 in the UK. That's the line item leadership sees.
- Pivot to the hidden cost. Most employees never fully use or even understand what they have. The investment sits there, paid for and unused.
- Frame the question Tarek asked Matt mid-conversation: is there a hidden cost on top of the visible one, sitting in the comms gap between what's offered and what's understood?
- Position the post: this is a diagnosis of the hidden cost and a working framework for closing the gap. Practical, drawn from a 25-year practitioner.
- Foreground that the underlying problem is not unique to benefits — it sits in any internal communication that nobody owns coordinating. Benefits is just where the cost is most visible.

---

### Section 1: What the Hidden Cost Actually Is (~300 words)

- Three forms the hidden cost takes (use two if the third can't be sourced cleanly):
  1. **Wasted spend on benefits employees don't know exist.** Use the concrete anecdote: a 12,000-employee UK client offering travel loans, with three employees using the benefit. Not because the benefit is irrelevant, but because nobody knows it's there.
  2. **Eroded trust from comms that read like a checkbox.** When benefits comms feel like they were written by a lawyer, employees don't ignore them anymore — they share them, mock them, screenshot them. The damage compounds when the next message is the one that actually matters. Reference the viral-pension-comms moment Matt described: ordinary employees posting government-issued pension communications online with "WTF" reactions.
  3. **Vendor-controlled narrative.** *Hold this section if Matt's "90% from vendors" stat can be sourced; otherwise cut to two forms.* If kept: when the company funds the benefit but the vendor sends the email, the vendor gets the credit and shapes the experience. The company loses control of the most-direct touchpoint with employees on a topic the company is paying for.

- Use Matt's quote on vendor reach: "If your vendors are louder than you, they're getting the credit. They're controlling the narrative."
- Close the section by framing the hidden cost in financial language leadership can act on: every dollar spent on a benefit that isn't understood is a dollar that bought nothing. (This line comes from the deck speaker note — paraphrase to fit the section, don't quote it directly here, save the direct version for the conclusion.)

### Section 2: Why It Happens — From Compliance to Connection (~350 words)

- Open by naming the maturity ladder Matt walks through in the conversation: compliance → clarity → relevance → connection. Use the deck-canonical descriptions.
  - **Compliance** — comms exists because regulation requires it. Boxes ticked, written by the cold hand of a lawyer.
  - **Clarity** — the team takes time to explain things in ways that help people understand what, when, and why it matters.
  - **Relevance** — the team thinks about the message from the audience's perspective and personalizes content to reflect different lives and known needs.
  - **Connection** — the team uses the comms moment to connect to hearts and minds. Transparency builds trust; language shows the company cares.
- Position most organizations as stuck somewhere between clarity and relevance. Diagnose the block: time and prioritization. HR and total rewards teams own the content but lack the bandwidth and skill set to coordinate. Internal comms generalists have a long queue of leadership and strategy comms ahead of benefits.
- Quote Tarek's challenge: "How did we get to a state where something so important is reduced to checking a box?"
- Bridge to a parallel: in marketing, no one would launch a campaign without measuring whether it worked. People comms — the largest line item in most company budgets at roughly 70% — gets a different standard. Push the reader to notice the asymmetry.
- Close the section by reframing what the ladder is asking for. It is not asking for more output. It is asking for a different posture toward the audience: from informing them to fighting for their attention.

---

### Section 3: The Four Moves to Fight for Attention (~400 words)

- Open with Matt's hill-to-die-on line: *"No one wakes up in the morning and thinks, 'I'm going to spend two and a half hours of my life cover to cover reading that benefits communication.'"*
- Frame the section: if the audience is not captive, every benefits message has to earn the open. Four practical moves, in deck order.

  **1. Make it interesting.** Use the Nana example — the agony-aunt grandma character built for a UK retailer's pensions campaign. Provocative, glamorous, relatable. Compare against the white-linen-couple-on-a-beach financial-wellness cliché. The point is not gimmickry; it is that the message stops being skippable.

  **2. Make it meaningful.** Connect the message to the moment the audience is in. Matt's example: "If you're up at one in the morning searching the internet about your kid's symptoms, we can help with that." Specific moments beat general benefit descriptions.

  **3. Make it emotive.** Real stories of people whose lives changed because of a benefit they used. Matt's example: a campaign anchored on an employee saying, "This lady had cancer living right on my face, and I didn't know about it." The plan was always there. The story is what made anyone use it.

  **4. Make it findable.** Comms content lives all over the place in most organizations — intranet, PDFs, vendor portals, email archives. A frictionless single point of entry, with search, filters, and tags, is the difference between a benefit that gets used and one that gets buried.

- Close the section by stitching the four moves together: interesting opens the door, meaningful holds attention, emotive earns trust, findable lets people act when they're ready.

---

### Section 4: Control the Message All Year (~300 words)

- Open with the structural problem: most benefits comms cycles look like 350 days of silence followed by a cacophony of annual-enrollment messages. Neither end of that pattern serves anyone.
- Introduce a 3-layer architecture for breaking the cycle:
  1. **Evergreen layer** — always-on content where employees know to go. Single point of entry, organized by life moments, not by plan administrator.
  2. **Constant drum beat** — repeated exposures. Stories that surface benefits in the flow of normal company communication. (Use the rule of seven exposures Matt referenced — people generally need to see a message multiple times before it registers.)
  3. **High-impact campaigns** — the Nana-style guerrilla moments, ideally tied to events outside enrollment so the comms doesn't always arrive in a rush.
- Add the audience-reach point most companies miss: 84 million employees are covered by benefits in the US, alongside 74 million dependents (per NBER). Nearly half of the people receiving benefits and making plan decisions are people the company has never communicated with directly. Reaching the home — through email captures, family-friendly content, or intentional inclusion of dependents in messaging — is most companies' largest under-served audience.

---

### Section 5: How to Know It's Working — the VALUE Framework (~250 words)

- Open with the budget-defense framing: open rates and click-throughs are vanity metrics that don't move budget conversations. To defend a benefits-comms investment, the team needs evidence the work is landing.
- Introduce the VALUE framework as a measurement structure:
  - **V**isibility — did employees see the message?
  - **A**ccessibility — could they get to the underlying content easily?
  - **L**iteracy — did they understand what was offered?
  - **U**sage — did they act on it?
  - **E**xperience — what was the felt quality of the interaction?
- Note that each dimension can be captured through some mix of analytics, pulse surveys, focus groups, or anecdotal feedback — and that the goal is a benchmark, not a perfect dashboard. Reference Cerkl Broadcast's analytics features as one way to surface visibility, accessibility, and usage data without manual reporting overhead.
- Close with Matt's measurement line: *"If you can't measure it, you can't defend it. You can't ask for more budget. You can't dedicate resource to it. You can't bring in outside help."* Frame measurement as a budget-defense tool, not a compliance exercise.
