### H1: SharePoint for Internal Communications — A 2026 Playbook

### Intro (100–150 words)

- Open by acknowledging SharePoint's footprint: most enterprise IC teams already have it, IT runs it, and it is the default intranet for the Microsoft 365 stack.
- Pivot to the gap: SharePoint is a content destination. It is not, by itself, a complete internal communications strategy. The post explains where the line falls and what to add.
- Include `SharePoint internal communications` in the first sentence or two.
- State the frame: SharePoint is where content can live; reaching the right employee at the right moment requires a delivery, personalization, and measurement layer on top.

---

### H2: Where SharePoint works well for internal communications

- The strengths are real and worth naming up front:
  - **Document and policy hub** — versioned, searchable, permissioned. The right home for HR documents, brand guidelines, process libraries, and reference content.
  - **Team and department sites** — natural home for project pages, departmental news, and team-owned content.
  - **Integration with Microsoft 365** — Teams, Outlook, OneDrive, and Power Automate live next to it. For organizations already on Microsoft, the integration story is short.
  - **Permissioned content** — content gated by group, role, or AD attribute is straightforward.
- Closing line: these strengths make SharePoint a good intranet. They do not make it a complete internal comms platform.

---

### H2: Where SharePoint falls short without personalization

- Frame the gap directly. The intranet is pull-based. Internal communications need both push and pull.
- Three concrete shortfalls:
  1. **Audience targeting by role, location, department, tenure, and communication need** — SharePoint can permission content, but personalizing what employees actually see when they land is much harder out of the box. The typical experience: every employee sees roughly the same home page, even when 70% of the content is irrelevant to them.
  2. **Relevance decay** — when every employee sees the same content, the audience stops trusting the intranet to surface what matters to them. Once relevance erodes, the channel itself loses trust.
  3. **Publishing-to-reach gap** — SharePoint tells you a page was published. It does not tell you, by audience, who actually saw the content, who engaged with it, or who missed it.
- Include the sibling link to `/blog/distributed-workforce` here, anchored on "the audiences SharePoint alone tends to miss."

---

### H2: What SharePoint personalization actually means

- Open with: `SharePoint personalization` is a term that means different things to different vendors. Here is what an internal comms team should actually ask for.
- Four practical components, written as prose with the four ideas embedded — not a bullet list dump:
  - **Personalized content surfaces** — each employee sees content scored against their role, location, department, and prior engagement, not a single home page.
  - **Rules-based audiences fed from real employee data** — segments built from HRIS or identity attributes, kept current automatically. Not a static distribution list.
  - **Integration with the employee record** — the same audience definition that targets an email targets SharePoint content, so the channels stay consistent.
  - **Delivery beyond the intranet** — personalized SharePoint content is reachable through email digests, mobile push, and chat tools, not only when an employee chooses to visit the intranet.
- Closing line: anything short of these four is publishing, not personalization.

### H2: The internal comms use cases SharePoint cannot solve alone

- Five use cases, each with one or two sentences of explanation. Prose-led with a clear anchor in each paragraph.
  1. **Urgent company updates** — a SharePoint post sitting on the intranet does not reach employees in real time. Layoffs, leadership changes, safety incidents, and outage notifications all require push, not pull.
  2. **Frontline or deskless reach** — frontline workers do not log into the corporate intranet on a regular cadence. Include sibling link to `/blog/mobile-workforce-communication` here, and the second sibling `/blog/gig-economy-workers-can-help-fill-skills-gap` for the nontraditional-workforce angle.
  3. **Leadership messages that need read confirmation** — read receipts, acknowledgments, and a record of who saw a CEO note all sit outside SharePoint's native capabilities.
  4. **Segmented campaigns across locations or business units** — a region-specific safety campaign, a single-business-unit policy change, or a country-specific benefits update each need granular targeting and channel-by-channel reporting.
  5. **Cross-channel performance reporting** — leadership wants to know whether the message landed, not whether the page was published. SharePoint alone does not produce that view.

### H2: How to extend SharePoint with email, Teams, mobile, and analytics

- Frame: layering, not replacing. Keep SharePoint as the destination for content that lives long. Add execution layers around it.
- Four extensions, written as prose with the names embedded:
  - **Email as the delivery push** — purpose-built internal email, not the marketing email tool, drives traffic to SharePoint content and reaches employees in their inbox.
  - **Teams and chat for in-flow visibility** — surface the same content where employees already spend time.
  - **Mobile push for deskless and frontline audiences** — a mobile app or SMS path covers the audiences who do not work at a desk.
  - **Cross-channel analytics** — one view of who saw the message across SharePoint, email, Teams, and mobile. Include cross-pillar link to `/broadcast/analytics/omni-channel-analytics` here.

### H2: A 30-day plan to improve SharePoint-based internal communications

- Practical close. Walk a comms team through what to do in the next 30 days, regardless of whether they buy a delivery tool.
- Four weekly moves, written as prose with the week names embedded:
  - **Week 1 — Audit the home page.** Count how many sections are personalized vs. global. Pull the SharePoint analytics on which pages get traffic and which sit at zero. Identify the top 10 pages by traffic and the bottom 20 by intent.
  - **Week 2 — Map your audiences.** Pull a clean list of segments from HRIS: role, location, department, tenure, frontline-vs-corporate. Match the segments to the content categories that exist. The mismatches are the gaps.
  - **Week 3 — Pick one urgent or segmented campaign that needs to leave SharePoint.** Send it through email and confirm read rates by segment. The point is not the campaign; the point is generating data on who you actually reached when SharePoint is not the only channel.
  - **Week 4 — Build the business case.** Use what week 3 produced to compare published-on-SharePoint reach against email-plus-SharePoint reach. The gap is the cost of intranet-only delivery, and it is the data leadership needs to fund the next move.

### FAQ section (4 questions)

1. **Is SharePoint good for internal communications?**
   SharePoint is a strong intranet and document hub, but it is a destination, not a complete internal communications platform. It does not natively handle real-time push, audience-personalized content surfaces, or cross-channel measurement. Most teams use SharePoint alongside a delivery layer that adds email, mobile, and analytics.

2. **What is SharePoint personalization?**
   SharePoint personalization is the practice of tailoring what each employee sees on SharePoint based on their role, location, department, and engagement history, rather than showing one home page to everyone. It depends on rules-based audiences fed by HRIS or identity data and, in practice, also on extending content delivery beyond the intranet itself.

3. **How do you reach frontline employees through SharePoint?**
   Frontline employees rarely log into the corporate intranet on a regular schedule. Reaching them requires extending SharePoint content into channels they actually use — mobile push, SMS, email, or chat — and tracking reach by segment so frontline coverage is visible alongside corporate coverage.

4. **What is the best way to measure SharePoint internal communications?**
   SharePoint's native analytics report page views and visits, but they do not report cross-channel reach, audience-segment performance, or whether a specific message landed with a specific group. Cross-channel analytics — one view across SharePoint, email, Teams, and mobile — is the practical answer, especially for comms leaders defending budget or program investment.
