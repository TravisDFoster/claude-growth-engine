### Introduction (75–100 words)

- Open with the gap: most internal communicators have access to more data than they ever have, and yet leadership reports often feel like they go into a black hole.
- The problem is rarely the data. It is the framing. A dashboard is not a report. A list of metrics is not a story.
- Frame the post: this is a guide to building a leadership-facing report that turns the data you already have into a narrative leadership remembers, references, and acts on.

---

### Section 1: Why a Metrics Dump Fails (125–150 words)

- The most common reporting mistake: paste open rates, click rates, send counts into a slide and call it done.
- This fails for three reasons:
  1. **No context.** A 38% open rate means nothing without a benchmark or a trend.
  2. **No interpretation.** Leaders should not have to decide what the number means — that is the communicator's job.
  3. **No story.** A list of numbers does not connect to anything leadership cares about.
- Reframe: leadership does not want to see your dashboard. They want to know what is working, what is not, and what you are doing about it.
- Reporting is a communication discipline, not a data export.

---

### Section 2: The Three-Part Structure of a Report That Lands (200–250 words)

Walk through a simple, repeatable structure communicators can use for any monthly or quarterly leadership report.

**1. The headline (one sentence)**
- Lead with the single most important takeaway. Not a metric — a conclusion.
- Example: "Q1 engagement held steady, but reach among frontline staff dropped 14% after we moved benefits comms to email-only."
- If leadership reads only one line, this is what they remember.

**2. The signal (3–5 metrics, with context)**
- Pick the small set of numbers that support the headline. Each one should answer a question leadership has, not just describe what happened.
- For each metric: include the number, a comparison (vs. last period or benchmark), and a one-line interpretation.
- Example: "Open rate: 47% (up from 42% last quarter). Driven by a more disciplined subject-line review process."
- Skip metrics that do not support the headline. Comprehensive is not the goal.

**3. The action (what changes next)**
- Close with the one or two things you are doing in response to what the data showed.
- This is what separates a status update from a strategic report. It also signals to leadership that the comms function is operating with intention, not just reacting.

---

### Section 3: What to Leave Out (125–150 words)

- More data is not a more credible report. It is usually a less credible one.
- Leave out:
  - **Vanity metrics** — total sends, total subscribers, anything that grows by default.
  - **Activity-only reporting** — "we sent 12 newsletters this quarter" tells leadership nothing about whether anything worked.
  - **Unfiltered dashboards** — if leadership has to interpret the data, the report has not done its job.
  - **Apologies for the data** — "we don't have great tracking for X" undermines the whole report. State what you know and what you are doing to close the gap.
- The sharper the filter, the more credible the communicator. A two-page report leadership reads is worth more than a ten-page report no one finishes.

---

### Section 4: A Reporting Cadence That Builds Credibility (100–125 words)

- One-off reports do not build trust. A consistent cadence does.
- Recommend: a brief monthly written summary (one page or short email) and a deeper quarterly review (slides or a longer doc with trends).
- The monthly keeps leadership oriented to current performance. The quarterly is where strategic conversations happen — what is working, where to invest, what to change.
- Over time, the cadence does the heavy lifting. Leadership stops asking "is comms working?" because the answer is in their inbox every month.
- This is also where comms functions earn the right to bigger conversations — budget, headcount, strategic involvement — without having to make the case from scratch each time.

- Summarize the core shift: leadership reporting is a separate discipline from running a comms program. Both require skill, and both compound.
- Reinforce that the data most communicators need is already in their tools. The work is in the framing, not the gathering.

## Additional Notes for Writer

- Tone: practical, peer-level, structural. The audience is experienced communicators who already pull data — they do not need to be sold on measurement, they need a sharper way to present it.
- Avoid overlap with the May 19 post on building a case for comms investment. That post is about one-off budget conversations; this post is about ongoing reporting cadence. If a reader has read both, they should feel like complementary tools, not duplicates.
- Use concrete example phrasings (like the "headline" example in Section 2) to make the structure tangible. Communicators learn faster from sample sentences than from abstract advice.
- Keep product mentions light — one natural reference in the conclusion is sufficient. This post earns trust by being useful, not promotional.
- Headers and short paragraphs throughout. The post itself should model the discipline of clear leadership communication.
