### Outline

**Introduction (75–100 words)**
- Open with the Mental Health Awareness Month moment (May): a natural prompt for HR communicators to evaluate how well they're reaching employees with wellness resources
- Surface the core tension: most organizations offer solid wellness and mental health benefits, but utilization is low — often because the right employees never saw the message
- Thesis: segmentation is how you close the gap between "we sent it" and "they saw it," and measurement is how you know it worked

---

**Section 1: The Problem — Wellness Emails Get Lost in the Noise (150–175 words)**
- Wellness communications often get sent as one-size-fits-all blasts to all-staff lists
- A benefits reminder sent to every employee is easy to ignore — it reads as generic, not relevant
- Specific groups have different needs: new hires may not know what EAP benefits exist, remote employees may not know about virtual mental health resources, frontline workers may not check email at all
- The result: low awareness, low utilization, and no signal about what happened after send
- Framing: it's not a content problem — the wellness programs are real and valuable — it's a targeting and delivery problem

---

**Section 2: How Segmentation Helps the Right Employees See Wellness Benefits (200–250 words)**
- Define audience segmentation briefly: the ability to send different versions of a message (or different messages entirely) to specific employee groups based on attributes like role, location, department, employment type, or tenure
- Walk through practical examples for Mental Health Awareness Month:
  - New hires (0–90 days): EAP intro, what mental health resources exist and how to access them
  - Remote/hybrid employees: virtual therapy options, mental health apps included in benefits
  - Frontline or deskless workers: mobile-first delivery, brief and accessible format
  - Managers: resources to support their teams, conversation guides for mental health check-ins
- Key point: segmentation doesn't require writing five separate campaigns from scratch — it can be one message with targeted variations by audience group
- Mention that dynamic segmentation (pulling live employee data from HRIS) keeps lists current without manual upkeep — employees added to a "new hire" group automatically receive the right message

---

**Section 3: Measuring Whether Your Wellness Comms Are Landing (175–225 words)**
- Sending is not the same as reaching; Mental Health Awareness Month is an opportunity to put measurement in place, not just check a box
- Core metrics to track for wellness communications:
  - Open rate: did people open it? (baseline awareness)
  - Click rate: did they engage with a link — a resource, a benefits portal, a scheduling page?
  - Reach by segment: which employee groups opened vs. which didn't? Gaps in reach tell you where to adjust
  - Retargeting: did non-openers get a follow-up nudge?
- Connect measurement to the campaign goal: if the wellness program includes an EAP with a scheduling link, click-through to that link is a meaningful signal of actual utilization intent
- Without this data, HR communicators can't tell leadership whether the investment in wellness programs is reaching employees — "we sent an email" is not a proof-of-impact story
- Transition: this is the moment — Mental Health Awareness Month is a visible, time-bound campaign that gives you a reason to build the measurement habit now

---

**Section 4: Putting It Together — A Simple Mental Health Awareness Month Comms Plan (100–125 words)**
- Practical, scannable guidance (numbered list or brief bullets):
  1. Identify your audience segments: new hires, remote employees, frontline workers, managers
  2. Map each segment to a relevant wellness message or resource
  3. Build variations once; deliver through segmented sends
  4. Track open rates and click rates by segment — not just overall
  5. Follow up with non-openers within the campaign window
  6. Report results to leadership: reach, engagement, utilization signals
- Framing: this doesn't have to be a complex multi-channel campaign; even email segmentation alone meaningfully improves relevance and outcomes

---

**Conclusion / CTA (75–100 words)**
- Restate the core idea: employees can't benefit from wellness programs they don't know about — and HR communicators can't prove impact without measurement
- Mental Health Awareness Month is a timely reason to audit how wellness communications are currently being sent and to build better habits going forward
- Soft CTA: link to a related resource (benchmark data, segmentation guide, or Foundations sign-up) — frame it as a practical next step, not a hard sell
- Tone: encouraging and practical, not urgent or alarming
