### Introduction (75–100 words)

- Open with the familiar failure: the all-staff wellness email that gets a 12% open rate and no measurable uptick in EAP enrollment or benefits utilization
- The problem is not the message — it is the delivery. Wellness programs are not one-size-fits-all, and the employees who need them most are often the least likely to see a generic blast
- Preview the post: why broad wellness emails underperform, how to segment by role, location, and eligibility, and what targeted delivery does for engagement on EAP and benefits programs

---

### Section 1: Why Broad Wellness Emails Underperform (150–175 words)

- An all-staff email treats a 55-year-old plant worker the same as a 28-year-old remote software engineer — same subject line, same content, same timing
- The result is low relevance, which drives low engagement: employees disengage from emails that feel generic because they know most of the content was not written for them
- Wellness is personal. EAP programs, mental health resources, parental leave, FSA/HSA options, chronic condition management — each of these resonates differently depending on life stage, job type, location, and enrollment status
- The additional problem: broadcast-to-everyone creates noise for employees who are not eligible, which trains them to skip future wellness sends entirely
- Transition: the fix is not better copywriting on a broad send — it is getting the right message to the right group in the first place

---

### Section 2: The Case for Segmentation in Wellness Outreach (175–200 words)

- Segmentation in internal comms means delivering a version of the message that matches the audience receiving it — not creating 12 separate emails by hand, but using audience data to filter who gets what
- The three most useful dimensions for wellness segmentation:
  - **Role / job type**: Frontline and deskless workers may have different EAP access, benefit eligibility, or wellness challenges than office-based employees. A warehouse team lead and a corporate HR generalist should not receive identical mental health resource emails.
  - **Location**: State-specific benefit plans, local clinic partnerships, return-to-office wellness perks, regional insurance carriers — location changes what is actually relevant and available
  - **Eligibility**: Full-time vs. part-time status, tenure, enrollment windows, HSA eligibility — sending FSA enrollment reminders to employees not enrolled in the right health plan wastes their attention and yours
- The downstream effect: when employees consistently receive wellness communications that apply to them, open rates improve, program enrollment increases, and the HR team's credibility as a trusted source grows

---

### Section 3: How to Build Wellness Segments Without a Manual Process (175–200 words)

- The barrier most HR communicators cite: "We don't have time to manage separate lists for every wellness campaign"
- Dynamic segmentation — pulling directly from HRIS data — eliminates the manual list problem. When employee data (role, location, status, tenure) lives in Workday, ADP, Paycor, or another system, segmented wellness sends can be built once and updated automatically as the workforce changes
- Practical examples of automated segment logic for wellness:
  - Full-time employees hired 90+ days ago → EAP enrollment reminder
  - Employees in states with specific parental leave laws → targeted parental benefits send
  - Frontline / non-desk employees → mobile-optimized mental health resource push, not a long email
  - Employees enrolled in high-deductible health plans → HSA contribution deadline reminder
- With the right tooling, this is not a heavier lift than a single all-staff email — it is the same effort with dramatically better targeting
- Brief mention: Cerkl Broadcast syncs directly with HRIS/IdP systems so segments stay current without manual intervention; wellness communicators can build these audiences once and reuse them across campaigns

### Section 4: What Targeted Wellness Delivery Does for Engagement (150–175 words)

- Engagement is the output, not the goal — but it is a proxy for whether the right employees are actually reaching the resources meant for them
- What HR communicators should track when segmenting wellness sends:
  - Open rate by segment (not just overall): which groups are consistently disengaged?
  - Click-through on program links: did employees actually take the next step — scheduling a counseling session, downloading a resource, completing enrollment?
  - Unsubscribe trends: spikes on wellness sends often signal relevance problems, not message quality problems
- The business case for leadership: an EAP program with 4% utilization is not evidence of low employee need — it may be evidence of low awareness or poor targeting. Better-targeted wellness comms that drive measurable utilization is a defensible ROI story
- When internal comms and HR share this kind of data, the conversation shifts from "did we send the email?" to "did the program actually reach people?"
