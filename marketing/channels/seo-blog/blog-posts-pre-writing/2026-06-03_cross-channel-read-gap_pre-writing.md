### H2 #1: What the cross-channel read gap is (Diagnosis arc — definition, ~140 words)

- Restate the definition in a second register, less abstract: a message published to four channels can be read on one, two, three, or four of them by any given employee. Single-channel reporting only counts reads from the channel it owns. Cross-channel attribution counts reads across surfaces and deduplicates on employee ID, so the report reflects unique audience reached rather than the sum of channel impressions.
- One paragraph on what the gap is **not**: it is not the same as low open rates, low engagement, or poor content; those are different problems. The cross-channel read gap is specifically a measurement gap — a structural undercount that opens when reporting tools see fewer channels than the organization publishes on.
- Close with the question the section sets up for H2 #2: if the gap is measurement, who has measured it?

### H2 #2: Why no public benchmark measures the cross-channel read gap (Diagnosis arc — the vacuum, ~210 words)

- Lead claim: as of 2026, no published cross-channel IC engagement benchmark exists. Every named annual IC benchmark in the field is single-channel — almost always email.
- Walk through the public-benchmark layer:
  - Vendor-published email benchmarks are single-channel by construction. (Subject-matter framing: name the *category* of vendor data, not specific vendors; per Blog Post Research Mode, no competitor stats are cited.)
  - The longest-running practitioner survey in the field — **Gallagher's *State of the Sector*, 17th edition (2026; 1,300+ IC practitioners across 40 countries)** — is strategy and structure focused, not telemetry anchored across channels. It tracks what practitioners do; it does not measure what employees read across surfaces.
  - Analyst evaluations of intranet platforms — **Forrester's *Intranet Platforms Wave* (Q2 2026; Cheryl McKinnon, 13 vendors, 35 reference customers)** — score vendor capability, not aggregate cross-channel engagement.
- Synthesize the vacuum: every layer of the public-benchmark stack — vendor data, practitioner survey, analyst evaluation — sits adjacent to the cross-channel read gap without measuring it. The absence is itself the finding. The cross-channel read gap is a category the field has not built the telemetry to fill.
- Close with the implication that motivates H2 #3: a measurement gap of unknown size sits underneath every leadership report on internal communications reach.

### H2 #3: Why the cross-channel read gap matters in 2026 (Diagnosis arc, ~250 words)

Three sub-sections. End of diagnosis arc.

#### H3: Compliance — receipt evidence is incomplete

- If a benefits change, safety bulletin, or policy update is the kind of message a regulator, auditor, or legal team might ask about later, single-channel reporting can leave the comms team unable to prove who saw it. Cross-channel attribution is the receipt.
- Without a public benchmark, every comms team is left to defend their own coverage without a reference point. The compliance posture of the field is each organization on its own.
- No proprietary number cited. The argument is conceptual.

#### H3: Equity — frontline workers get less exposure than office staff

- Office workers default to email. Frontline and deskless employees default to mobile or Teams, often because they do not have a corporate inbox they check during their shift.
- Anchor with industry-wide primary research: Gallup's 2026 *State of the Global Workplace* puts engagement at 17% for on-site non-remote-capable workers versus 30% for exclusively-remote workers, a 13-percentage-point gap and the widest cohort split in the report. Forrester's Q2 2026 *Intranet Platforms Wave* names frontline-worker capabilities — shift management integration, personal device apps, do-not-disturb, task tools — as a top-line evaluation criterion for the first time, reflecting analyst recognition that reaching the frontline is structurally different work.
- Tie back to the gap: measure email only, and the frontline cohort is systematically underweighted in every reach metric, regardless of how good the writing is. The Gallup and Forrester anchors describe the workforce-level pattern; the cross-channel read gap is the per-message version of the same pattern.

#### H3: Engagement scoring — single-channel reads can inflate or deflate apparent reach

- A 60% email open rate looks healthy. Without cross-channel attribution, the comms team does not know whether the other 40% read the message on Teams, mobile, or the intranet, or whether they did not read it anywhere.
- The gap cuts both ways: single-channel reporting can undercount reach (employees read on a channel the report does not cover) or overcount reach (the same employee opens on multiple channels and separate dashboards each count the read).
- Link `/blog/internal-comms-metrics-that-matter` here.

### H2 #4: What you would need to see the cross-channel read gap (Operational arc, ~210 words)

- Frame: this is the shape of the right measurement, not a list of findings. The post deliberately does not publish proprietary numbers in v3.
- Two pieces are required:
  - **Channel-attribution rules.** A read is counted with a channel-appropriate signal — open events on email, post-views on Teams, push acknowledgments on mobile, page-views on intranet. None of these signals is perfect on its own. Pixel-tracked events on every channel carry known inflation patterns: preview-pane fires, automated security scanners, stale distribution lists. Any cross-channel measurement layer has to acknowledge per-channel artifacts even when it deduplicates employees across surfaces. (Anchor `[[open-rate-inflation]]` as generic methodology context.)
  - **Segmentation cuts that surface the structural patterns.** Role-segmented reads (an HR manager and a frontline supervisor live on different channel mixes). Geographic skews (regional channel preferences are usually larger than communicators expect). Tenure cuts (newer employees lean on mobile and chat; longer-tenured employees often live in email). A reach number that does not slice across these dimensions is averaging away the signal.
- Conceptual takeaway: cross-channel measurement is not a feature; it is a category of telemetry that requires an identity-resolved view across surfaces. The tooling layer is also lagging — Forrester's 2026 Wave found roughly half of intranet-platform reference customers (17 of 35) actively using their vendor's AI features despite heavy investment, evidence that the gap between what vendors ship and what teams operationalize is wider than the marketing suggests.
- Close with the bridge to H2 #5: a platform that publishes across the right channels and resolves identity across them is the visibility lens for what the public benchmark layer leaves unmeasured.

### H2 #6: Three actions IC teams can take in the next 30 days to close the cross-channel read gap (Operational arc — primary keyword in H2 — ~230 words)

This is the "what do I do on Monday" section. Three named actions. Preserve v2.1's actions; they were strong and TODO-free.

1. **Audit your reporting against your channel footprint.** Make two lists. The first is every channel your organization publishes internal messages on. The second is every channel your current reporting tool covers. The gap between those lists is your structural blind spot. The audit takes an hour and changes the leadership conversation. Link `/blog/how-to-measure-communication-effectiveness`.
2. **Build one cross-channel attribution view by hand.** Pick a single recurring send — CEO update, benefits comm, safety bulletin — and reconstruct cross-channel reads for one cycle, even if the reconstruction is a spreadsheet built from four channel exports. The exercise reveals the size of your own read gap on a message that already matters. The medium-term fix is a platform that does it natively — link `/broadcast/analytics` and `/broadcast/omni-channel`.
3. **Stop reporting channel-by-channel in leadership meetings.** Move to a single deduplicated audience reach number. It is harder to produce than four separate channel charts, and it is the only number that maps to the question leadership asks: did this message land with the people it was meant for?

Close with one forward-moving sentence about the credibility shift that follows when reporting reflects how messages move through the organization, not one channel at a time.
