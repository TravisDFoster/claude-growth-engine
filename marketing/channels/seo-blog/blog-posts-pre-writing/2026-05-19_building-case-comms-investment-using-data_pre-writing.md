### Introduction (75–100 words)

- Open with the tension: internal communicators often feel the impact of their work but struggle to articulate it in language that moves budget conversations forward.
- Acknowledge the asymmetry — comms teams know what good looks like, but leadership speaks a different language (efficiency, risk, retention, productivity).
- Frame the post: this is a practical guide to translating engagement data into a business case leadership can act on.

---

### Section 1: What Metrics Leadership Actually Cares About (150–175 words)

- Leadership is not unmoved by data — they are unmoved by the *wrong* data.
- Reframe common comms metrics in business terms:
  - Open rate → reach (are employees actually receiving critical information?)
  - Click rate / engagement → content relevance and action taken
  - Read receipts / acknowledgments → compliance and liability reduction
  - Segment reach → equity of access (are all teams getting the same critical info, including frontline?)
- Introduce the three categories leadership typically responds to:
  1. **Risk and compliance** — what happens when employees miss critical communications?
  2. **Productivity and time** — how much time is wasted on repeat communications, confusion, or escalations?
  3. **Retention and culture** — what does poor internal comms cost in turnover or disengagement?
- Keep metrics concrete. Reference industry benchmarks where available (e.g., 54% open rates vs. typical email client rates).

---

### Section 2: How to Frame Engagement Data as Business Impact (175–225 words)

- The most common mistake: presenting a metrics dashboard without a narrative.
- Walk through a simple translation framework:
  - Start with the metric → connect it to a business outcome → quantify or estimate the impact
  - Example: "Our open rate dropped 18% among warehouse staff during the benefits enrollment window. That likely contributed to a 12% spike in HR support tickets and 40+ hours of staff time resolving questions that a targeted re-send could have prevented."
- Three framing techniques:
  1. **Before/after comparison** — show movement over time (especially after a new tool or process was introduced)
  2. **Cost of inaction** — estimate what happens without investment (e.g., HR overhead, turnover risk, compliance gaps)
  3. **Benchmarking** — compare internal rates to industry standards to contextualize whether current performance is acceptable
- Emphasize: you do not need perfect data to make a compelling case. A well-framed directional argument is often more persuasive than a complex model.

### Section 3: A Step-by-Step Structure for the Comms ROI Conversation (200–250 words)

- Present a repeatable structure communicators can use before walking into a budget conversation:

**Step 1: Know the ask**
- Be specific: headcount, tool budget, additional license seats, or dedicated time. Vague asks die in committee.

**Step 2: Anchor to a business problem leadership already owns**
- Don't start with "comms needs better tools." Start with: "We had three missed compliance deadlines last quarter because messages weren't reaching field teams. Here's what that cost us."
- Identify a problem the exec already cares about — then connect it to a comms gap.

**Step 3: Show the current state with data**
- Pull what you have: send volume, open/click rates, audience coverage, ticket volume, survey feedback.
- Even rough data tells a story if you frame it well.

**Step 4: Model the improved state**
- Describe what better looks like and what it would take to get there.
- Use conservative estimates. Overclaiming erodes credibility.

**Step 5: Quantify the investment vs. the cost of the status quo**
- Frame the ask in terms of ROI, not cost. "This tool pays for itself if it prevents two compliance incidents per year" lands differently than "the license is $X."

## Additional Notes for Writer

- Tone: practical, peer-level, not preachy. The audience is experienced communicators who feel undervalued, not beginners who need basics.
- Avoid jargon like "synergy" or vague productivity platitudes. Be specific.
- Where possible, use concrete examples (even hypothetical ones framed as "for example") to make the framework tangible.
- The post should feel like a toolkit, not a lecture. Headers, short paragraphs, and a numbered step section support scannability.
