# Stop Guessing About Your Analytics: 5 Questions Every Internal Communicator Should Answer

The all-staff goes out, the open rate looks fine, and the team moves on to the next thing. Then a leader asks whether the message actually landed, and the honest answer is a shrug dressed up as a number. We sent it, most people opened it, so it probably worked. That is not measurement. That is hope with a dashboard attached.

Most of us have lived in that gap at some point. You have analytics, sometimes more than you can read, but you cannot reliably answer the question your CHRO or CFO is really asking: did this work, and how do you know? The problem is rarely a shortage of data. It is that no one has decided which questions the data is supposed to answer. Five questions do most of the work. A communicator who can answer all five stops guessing about whether their comms work and starts making a case leadership believes.

Here is the part worth saying up front, because it is the part most people don't believe: you do not need to be a data analyst to answer these. You need the right five questions and a tool that doesn't make you fight it. That's the whole game.

## Question 1: Who received the message?

Reach is the question most teams think they have covered and most teams get wrong, because they read it off an org-wide average. A 40 percent open rate tells you nothing about whether the plant floor saw the safety notice or whether the new hires got the benefits deadline. "I never got that email" is a sentence you should be able to answer with certainty, not a debate.

The fix is to break reach out by the groups that matter for the message in front of you, then look for the population that consistently falls below the rest. That group is your reach gap, and it stays invisible as long as you only watch the average. Audience visibility starts before the send, not after the complaint. Until you can name who reliably receives what you send, every number downstream is built on a guess about the denominator.

## Question 2: Who is engaged, and who isn't?

Averages hide stories. A 60 percent open rate can look healthy while quietly burying the fact that frontline staff, new hires, and one regional office are barely engaging at all. The single company-wide number is the enemy of actually knowing anything.

The move is to compare groups and look for the meaningful differences. The Albany office against the company as a whole. Managers against individual contributors. New hires against everyone who has been around longer than a year. Engagement is not one rate; it is a set of stories about who is leaning in and who has tuned out, and you can only act on the version that is broken out far enough to point at something.

## Question 3: When should I send for maximum impact?

Best send time is a question of audience behavior, not office opinion. The 9 a.m. Tuesday rule someone read in a blog post once is not a strategy. Your own people are telling you, in their open and click patterns, when they actually have attention to give you.

Read the behavior for the audience you care about, then place your message where they already are. A regional team that opens everything at the end of their shift is telling you when to send. A group that never touches anything on a Monday is telling you when not to. This is one of the easiest wins to grab, because the data already exists. You just have to look at it before you schedule, not after.

## Question 4: What content actually resonates?

After a few months of sends, patterns are sitting in the data that almost no one looks for. Think of categories and clicks as employee votes: every subscription, preference, and click is a small signal about what people actually want from you. Certain topics consistently pull people in. Certain formats die on arrival. Some content keeps earning opens weeks after it went out.

That last one matters more than teams realize. The newsletter item that quietly keeps getting read a month later is worth more than the splashy announcement that spiked and vanished. When you can see long-term value instead of judging every send in isolation, you stop guessing at the editorial calendar and start building it around what employees return to. Doing less of what does not work is usually the fastest way to lift the numbers that do.

## Question 5: What should I improve next?

Data is not the destination. Better communication is. The first four questions tell you what happened; this one is about what you do with it. Which categories are underperforming? Which audiences are you overcommunicating to? Which communicators on your team are quietly outperforming everyone else, and what are they doing that the rest could copy?

This is the question that moves you from message sender to strategic communication advisor. You surface the underperforming and the untapped, you benchmark across your own communicators to spread what works, and you walk into the next planning conversation with a recommendation instead of a recap. That is what it looks like to defend your function with data leadership actually believes, rather than a screenshot of a dashboard and a hopeful tone.

## Why these five, and why now

Leadership expectations have moved. Internal comms is increasingly asked to prove impact in the same language as every other function, which means the era of sending content and hoping it worked is closing. The teams that thrive are the ones who can answer who received it, who engaged, when to send, what resonates, and what to improve next, in minutes rather than in a scramble before a board meeting.

And here is the punchline: this is not the hard part anymore. The reporting that used to require a data analyst is now something you can run yourself, which is exactly why the tooling matters as much as the questions. Leadership-worthy reporting without analytics skills is not a someday goal. It is the bar, and it is reachable.
