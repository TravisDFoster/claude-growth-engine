# Mental Health Awareness Month: Communicating Wellness Programs with Segmentation

May is Mental Health Awareness Month, which means many HR communicators are either sending wellness emails right now or asking themselves why they haven't yet. Before the send button gets pressed, sit with a more useful question: are these messages reaching the employees who need them? Most organizations have substantive mental health benefits in place. The issue is rarely the program itself. Employees don't know about it, can't find it, or received a message that felt like it was written for someone else. Segmentation is how you close the gap between "we sent it" and "they saw something relevant." Measurement is how you know it worked.

## The Problem: Wellness Emails Get Lost Because They're Written for Everyone

A benefits reminder addressed to all 3,000 employees is easy to skip. Employees do care about their mental health. Generic all-staff messages train people to tune out. When every message reads the same, none of them feel urgent or personal.

The mismatch goes deeper than tone. Different employee groups have different needs around mental health resources. A new hire three weeks into their first month may have no idea that an Employee Assistance Program (EAP) exists, let alone how to access it. A remote employee working across time zones may not know that their benefits include virtual therapy options covered at the same rate as in-person visits. A frontline worker on a warehouse floor probably does not check email during a shift, and a manager overseeing a distributed team needs something different from what you would send to an individual contributor.

When a single message tries to speak to all of those situations at once, it ends up speaking to none of them well. Low open rates and low utilization of wellness programs are often treated as employee disengagement problems, but they are frequently employee wellness program communication problems. The programs are there. The employees never got a message that felt like it was for them.

## How Segmentation Helps the Right Employees See Wellness Benefits

Internal communications segmentation means sending different messages, or targeted variations of the same message, to specific employee groups based on attributes like role, location, department, employment type, or tenure. For Mental Health Awareness Month, it is the practical mechanism that makes mental health benefits employee communication land.

Here is what that looks like in practice. New hires in their first 90 days should receive a message focused on EAP basics: what the program is, how to access it, what it covers, and that it is confidential. This audience may have heard a one-minute overview during onboarding and nothing since. Remote and hybrid employees benefit from messaging that names virtual mental health resources (teletherapy options, mental health apps, digital wellness tools) because without that specificity, they may assume those options are not available or covered. Frontline and deskless workers need mobile-first delivery and short-form content. A three-paragraph email sent to a corporate inbox will not reach someone working a floor shift. Managers are a distinct segment too. They need resources focused on how to support their teams, including conversation guides for checking in on employees and language for navigating sensitive situations.

None of this requires writing five separate campaigns from scratch. Build one core message and vary it for each audience, with different opening sentences, different resource links, and different calls to action depending on what that group needs. The content investment is modest. The targeting difference is significant.

Dynamic segmentation takes this further. When audience lists are connected to live employee data from an HRIS system, segments like "employees hired in the last 90 days" or "employees at field locations" update automatically. New employees get added to the right group without manual list maintenance, and the right message finds them during the period when it is most useful.

## Measuring Whether Your Wellness Comms Are Landing

Sending is not the same as reaching. This matters during a visible campaign like Mental Health Awareness Month, when there is both organizational attention and a clear time window. The measurement habits you build now carry forward into how you communicate wellness programs the rest of the year.

Four metrics are worth tracking for any wellness email campaign. Open rate is the baseline: did employees open the message? Click rate tells you whether anyone engaged with an actual resource, whether that is a link to the EAP scheduling page, a mental health app signup, or a benefits portal. Reach by segment is where the insight lives: which employee groups opened the message, and which ones did not? Gaps in segment-level reach point to where the targeting or delivery approach needs to change. Retargeting closes the loop: did employees who did not open the first send receive a follow-up within the campaign window?

If the wellness program includes an EAP with a scheduling link, click-through to that link is a meaningful proxy for utilization intent. It will not tell you how many employees booked an appointment, but it tells you more than an all-staff open rate does about whether the communication moved anyone toward action.

Without this data, telling leadership whether the investment in wellness programs is reaching employees is hard. "We sent a Mental Health Awareness Month email" is not a proof-of-impact story. "We reached 84% of employees across segments, with our new hire cohort showing the highest click-through rate to the EAP portal" is. That kind of reporting matters both for demonstrating the value of the comms function and for making the case to sustain or expand wellness benefits investment.

## Putting It Together: A Simple Mental Health Awareness Month Comms Plan

This does not need to be a complex multi-channel production to be effective. Even email segmentation alone improves relevance and outcomes compared to a single all-staff blast. A practical starting point looks like this:

First, identify your audience segments: new hires, remote or hybrid employees, frontline or deskless workers, and managers. Second, map each segment to the wellness message or resource most relevant to where they are. Third, build the core message once and create targeted variations for each group. Fourth, track open rates and click rates by segment, not just overall. Fifth, follow up with non-openers within the campaign window with a shorter, more direct send. Sixth, document the results (reach, engagement, and any utilization signals you can connect) and bring that data to leadership.

Mental Health Awareness Month gives you a timely reason to build this process now. The same segmentation logic and measurement habits apply to open enrollment, benefits reminders, and every other communication where the goal is more than a checkbox.

Employees cannot benefit from wellness programs they do not know about. HR communicators cannot prove the impact of their work without data. Both problems are solvable with the same approach: send the right message to the right people, then measure what happened.
