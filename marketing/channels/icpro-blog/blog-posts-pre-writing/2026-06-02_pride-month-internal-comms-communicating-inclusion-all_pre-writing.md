## Outline for Writing

### H1: Pride Month Internal Comms: Communicating Inclusion All Year, Not Just June

Opening (1–2 paragraphs): Most IC teams treat Pride Month as a once-a-year visibility moment. Banner on the intranet, rainbow logo, a CEO statement, an ERG spotlight or two. Then the calendar flips to July and inclusion communication goes quiet for eleven months. The visibility is fine. The problem is that the rest of the year carries the actual program, and most teams have no plan for it. Pride Month is a useful test case for whether an organization runs an inclusion communications program or just a June campaign.

### H2: The Difference Between a Moment and a Program

- A moment is visible, time-boxed, scripted. A program is continuous, segmented, measurable.
- Why "rainbow-washing" is a comms infrastructure problem, not a values problem — most teams want to do more but only have moments built into the calendar
- The IC tell: if your inclusion content calendar has 4 LGBTQ+ touchpoints in June and 0 between July and the following May, you are running a moment
- Year-round inclusion communication is not 12 themed months stacked together — it is the policy comms, the leadership comms, the operational comms, and the ERG visibility that keep running between heritage months

### H2: What Always-On Inclusion Comms Actually Looks Like

- Policy and benefits comms: when transition-related benefits, parental leave for same-sex couples, or partner benefits change, the comms strategy treats them as inclusion comms, not just HR comms
- Leadership comms that reference LGBTQ+ employees in the ordinary course — not just during Pride
- ERG visibility outside June: ERG-authored content, ERG-led learning sessions, ERG voices in regular cadence comms (not just the heritage month spotlight)
- Operational signals: pronouns in email signatures supported by guidance not mandates, inclusive language in benefits enrollment comms, accessible event comms by default
- The framing shift: stop asking "what do we do for Pride Month" and start asking "what does our LGBTQ+ employee experience look like across the comms calendar"

### H2: Common Pitfalls

- Rainbow-washing without follow-through (the external logo swap, no internal program behind it)
- June-only ERG visibility — the LGBTQ+ ERG appears in June and disappears until the next June
- Treating Pride as a marketing moment rather than an internal comms moment (external campaign runs, internal cadence does not)
- ERG co-creation done as approval, not authorship — the comms team writes it, the ERG reviews it, employees can tell
- Missing the rest-of-year touchpoints because the IC team does not own the inclusion content calendar (HR or DEI owns it, IC distributes it, nobody is responsible for cadence)

### H2: A Practical Year-Round Framework

A simple four-part frame IC teams can adopt:

1. **Visibility moments** — Pride Month, Coming Out Day, Trans Day of Visibility, World AIDS Day. Time-boxed. ERG-co-authored. Targeted to opted-in audiences where the content is community-specific.
2. **Operational continuity** — policy, benefits, leadership, and ERG visibility comms that keep running on the regular IC cadence between visibility moments. This is where most programs fall short.
3. **Listening loops** — pulse questions, ERG feedback channels, segment-level engagement data that tells you whether inclusion comms is landing differently across employee groups.
4. **Calendar discipline** — the IC team owns the inclusion comms calendar across all 12 months, not just June. Without that, "year-round" is aspirational language with no operating model behind it.

### H2: How to Measure Whether Inclusion Comms Is Landing Across Segments

- Reach by employee population: are LGBTQ+ ERG-targeted communications reaching opted-in employees, and are broader inclusion communications reaching the full workforce?
- Engagement gaps by segment: a consistent open or click gap between LGBTQ+ ERG members and the general employee population on inclusion content is a signal, not a vanity metric
- Opt-in / opt-out patterns on ERG-targeted content over time
- Pulse feedback on belonging tied to specific employee populations, not org-wide averages
- The point: org-wide engagement averages hide the inclusion question. Segment-level engagement is the only way to know if the comms is landing where it needs to.

### Closing: Stop Designing the June Campaign First

Forward-moving takeaway. The teams doing this well design the year-round operating model first and let the June moment sit inside it. The teams doing this poorly design June first and try to extend it. The order matters because it determines whether the rest of the year exists at all.
