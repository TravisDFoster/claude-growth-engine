## Outline for Writing

**Working H1 (post-title field in Wix):** How to Benchmark Open and Click Rates for Employee Email

**Opening (1–2 paragraphs):**
- Most IC teams compare their open rates to marketing benchmarks and either celebrate or panic for the wrong reasons.
- Internal email is a different game. The right way to benchmark is against internal-comms norms, against the gap between open and click, and against your own segmented audiences.

**H2: What "good" looks like for internal email open rates**
- Healthy internal open rates run higher than marketing email — typical range 40–60%, with strong programs landing 60%+.
- Why the floor is higher: pre-existing relationship, work-relevant content, distribution to known employees rather than a permission-based marketing list.
- Caveat on measurement methodology — open-rate inflation from preview panes and image-blocking, and how to read the number with that in mind.

**H2: What "good" looks like for click rates**
- Internal click rates typically range 8–20% depending on content type. News digests, surveys, and acknowledgment-required emails sit at the higher end.
- A 5% click rate on a CEO update isn't a failure if the message was a single read-and-go announcement. Match the benchmark to the job.

**H2: Reading the gap between open and click**
- The open-to-click ratio is more useful than either number on its own.
- High open + low click = the subject line worked, the content didn't earn the next action.
- Low open + high click = small but engaged audience; you have a reach problem, not a relevance problem.
- Both low = either wrong audience or wrong topic.
- Both high = working as designed; protect what's working before you change anything.

**H2: How segmentation changes every benchmark**
- A company-wide open rate is an average that hides the truth. Frontline workers, HQ staff, and field teams behave differently.
- Segment by location, role, and tenure before benchmarking. New hires open at higher rates than 5-year tenured employees; deskless workers open later in the day or not at all.
- Without segmentation, you're benchmarking a blended number that doesn't match anyone's actual experience.

**H2: Building your own internal benchmark over time**
- The best benchmark for your program is your own rolling 12-month baseline by content type and audience segment.
- Industry numbers are useful for sanity checks, not for goal-setting.
- Track three things: open rate by segment, click rate by content type, and the open-to-click ratio. Compare new sends against your own baseline before comparing to anyone else.

**Closing paragraph:**
- Benchmarking isn't about hitting a number. It's about knowing whether the number you got tells you to keep going, fix the subject line, fix the content, or fix the list.
