## Outline for writing

### H1
What Inclusive Internal Comms Actually Looks Like (Beyond Box-Checking)

### Opening (2 paragraphs)
Direct answer up front: inclusive internal comms is a practical discipline, visible in editorial decisions, staffing of the planning conversation, what gets measured, and what runs between the visible moments. Box-checking is also visible — it shows up in the same four places, just differently. The rest of the post is a diagnostic across those four layers.

### H2: How to spot box-checking in your own program
The patterns that signal a program is running on box-checking rather than discipline:
- Calendar is driven entirely by external heritage-month dates with no continuity in between
- One channel (usually all-staff email or the intranet homepage) carries every inclusion message regardless of audience
- ERG involvement is approval-stage review, not planning-stage authorship
- The language gets audited; the channel mix and audience reach do not
- Success is measured by whether the message went out, not by who received it

These are not bad-faith failures. They are what happens when inclusion comms sits inside an editorial process designed for general-audience announcements. Naming the patterns is the first move.

### H2: What inclusive internal comms looks like in the editorial process
The editorial brief is where discipline shows up or does not. The questions a brief surfaces in a substantive program:
- Who is this for, specifically
- Who is missing from this audience
- Through what channel and at what cadence will they receive it
- What would make this land as real vs. read as performance

These questions sit before the draft, not after. In a box-checking program, they show up in the review cycle if they show up at all, and by then the content has already been shaped to the default audience and default channel.

ERG involvement is the cleanest editorial tell. In a discipline-driven program, ERGs are co-authors at the planning stage and the IC team's role is editorial production. In a box-checking program, ERGs are reviewers of content already written, and the review is a sign-off step rather than a shaping step.

### H2: Who is in the room — and who is not
Staffing of the planning conversation matters more than wording on the page. The question worth asking on every inclusion brief: who is missing from this conversation that should be in it?

- Frontline and deskless population perspectives are present at the planning stage, not added as a deliverable later
- Senior leaders are part of inclusion comms cadence between heritage months, not only during them
- The IC team is the accountable owner of the inclusion content calendar, not a downstream distributor for HR or DEI
- ERG leads are funded and resourced for the planning time, not asked to volunteer review hours on top of their day jobs

Where a comms calendar consistently lacks one of these inputs, the gap shows up in the content months later.

### H2: What inclusive comms programs actually measure
Org-wide averages hide the inclusion question. A 35 percent open rate on a heritage-month post tells the team almost nothing about whether the content reached the population it was meant for. The metrics that separate substance from box-checking:

- Reach and engagement by employee segment, not org-wide
- Engagement gaps between a specific community and the broader workforce on community-relevant content
- Opt-in and opt-out patterns on community-targeted comms over time
- Pulse feedback on belonging tied to specific populations, not averaged across the org

A box-checking program measures sends. A discipline-driven program measures reach gaps, and treats a consistent gap as a signal to investigate rather than a footnote in a report.

### H2: The discipline between the visible moments
The clearest tell sits in the calendar between heritage months. What does the inclusion comms calendar show in October, in February, in August? In a discipline-driven program, the calendar shows policy comms framed as inclusion comms, leadership communications that reference inclusion in the ordinary course, ERG visibility in the regular IC cadence, and feedback loops running on their own rhythm independent of any external date.

In a box-checking program, the calendar shows four to six visible moments per year, separated by months of silence on inclusion topics. That gap is the program. The visible moments are what gets posted about externally; the gap is where inclusion comms either exists or does not.

### Closing paragraph (forward-moving takeaway)
The diagnostic is simple. Look at the editorial brief, the planning room, the measurement report, and the calendar between heritage months. The same four places show whether inclusion comms is operating as a discipline or running as a sequence of box-checking moments. The fix is rarely a better campaign. It is a better operating model, and it is mostly visible in places the campaign itself does not touch.
