# One-Pager Writing Skill

When the user asks to write or create a one-pager, follow this process in order. Do not skip steps. Do not begin drafting until steps 1–5 are complete.

---

## Step 1 — Intake: Ask the brief questions

Ask the user these questions before doing anything else. You can ask them all at once.

- What is this one-pager for? (event, sales meeting, campaign, website download, etc.)
- Is there a deadline or in-hands date?
- Who requested it, and who owns final approval?

---

## Step 2 — Audience: Confirm the target reader

Ask the user:

- Who is the primary reader? (role, industry, company size)
- Where are they in the buying journey? (unaware, evaluating, close to decision)
- What does the reader already know about Cerkl — do they need context set, or is this for someone mid-funnel or later?

Cross-reference against `/Users/travisfoster/claude-code/cerkl/shared/icp.md` to confirm fit and flag any misalignment.

---

## Step 3 — Goal: Lock the job this one-pager is doing

Ask the user:

- What is the one action or belief shift you want the reader to have after reading this?
- Which of these best describes the job: awareness, education, objection handling, sales enablement, or event handout?

Do not proceed until there is a single, clear answer. If the user gives multiple goals, flag the conflict and ask them to pick a primary.

---

## Step 4 — Core message architecture

Before drafting, confirm the skeleton with the user. Present it for approval:

- **Headline claim**: the single most important thing the reader should believe
- **Supporting points** (2–3): what proves the claim
- **Proof** for each point: stat, case study reference, or customer quote
- **CTA**: what the reader should do next

If the user can't supply proof points, surface available proof from the content sources in Step 5 and ask them to confirm.

---

## Step 5 — Content sourcing: Load context before writing

Before drafting, read the following files and use them to inform positioning, messaging, and proof points:

- `/Users/travisfoster/claude-code/cerkl/shared/company-info.md` — company overview, product description, key differentiators
- `/Users/travisfoster/claude-code/cerkl/shared/icp.md` — ideal customer profile, pain points, value drivers
- `/Users/travisfoster/claude-code/cerkl/shared/competitors.md` — competitive landscape, how Cerkl is positioned against alternatives

Also ask the user:

- Are there any existing decks, case studies, or product docs I should pull from?
- Is there a specific customer story or stat that must be included?

---

## Step 6 — Structure and space constraints

Confirm the section skeleton and word count targets with the user before writing. Default structure:

| Section | Word target |
|---|---|
| Headline | 8–12 words |
| Subhead / problem statement | 20–30 words |
| Solution overview | 40–60 words |
| Supporting points (2–3) | 25–40 words each |
| Proof / social proof | 20–30 words |
| CTA | 10–15 words |

Ask: Does the design have a fixed template, or is structure flexible? If a template exists, adjust the word counts to match.

---

## Step 7 — Draft

Write the one-pager using the approved message architecture from Step 4 and the word targets from Step 6. Follow the voice and tone guidance from the marketing brand guidelines.

Present the draft section by section so the user can react to each before committing to the full piece.

---

## Step 8 — Review

After presenting the draft:

- Ask the user for feedback by section
- Flag any sections that exceed word targets
- Limit to two rounds of revisions before asking the user to lock the copy

---

## Step 9 — Design handoff

Once copy is approved, deliver it in this format:

- Plain text, labeled by section name
- Word count noted for each section
- Any locked phrases or required language called out explicitly (e.g., legal language, product names, required taglines)

Ask the user where the final copy file should be saved.
