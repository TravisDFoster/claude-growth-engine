# Strategy Principles for B2B SaaS

A quick-reference guide for writing effective business and marketing strategy, distilled from Richard Rumelt's *Good Strategy Bad Strategy*.

---

## The Core Distinction

**Strategy is not a goal. Strategy is how you achieve a goal.**

"Grow revenue 30% this year" is a goal. "Win mid-market HR teams by owning the employee communication category before competitors do" is a strategy.

If you can't explain *how* you'll get somewhere — specifically, what you'll do differently than you're doing now — you don't have a strategy yet.

---

## The Three-Part Kernel of Good Strategy

Every good strategy has three components. If one is missing, it's incomplete.

### 1. Diagnosis
Name the specific challenge or obstacle standing between you and the goal. Be honest and precise.

> "Our free trial converts at 4% because users don't reach their first successful send within 14 days."

Not: *"We need to grow faster."*

The diagnosis determines everything else. A vague diagnosis produces a vague strategy.

### 2. Guiding Policy
A clear rule or principle that directs how you'll address the diagnosis. It rules things *out* — it tells you what you won't do as much as what you will.

> "We will reduce friction in the first 14 days above all else — no new features until activation improves."

### 3. Coherent Actions
A set of specific, coordinated steps that all point in the same direction. Each action should reinforce the others.

> - Redesign onboarding flow to surface first send in under 10 minutes  
> - Create an in-app checklist tied to activation milestones  
> - Assign a CSM to all trials over 500 employees  
> - Update all trial nurture emails to focus on time-to-value, not feature announcements

## The Four Hallmarks of Bad Strategy

Use these as a gut check before publishing any strategy doc.

### 1. All Goals, No How
Lists ambitious targets with no explanation of *how* they'll be achieved. Common in OKRs and board decks.

> Bad: *"Become the #1 employee communications platform in North America."*  
> Fix: What specifically will you do — that you aren't doing now — to get there?

### 2. Fluff
Vague, impressive-sounding language that says nothing. Often produced by committee.

> Bad: *"We will leverage our best-in-class platform to deliver synergistic communication experiences."*  
> Fix: Replace every abstract noun with a specific, falsifiable claim.

### 3. No Defined Problem
A plan that doesn't name what it's solving. If you skip the diagnosis, no one can evaluate whether the plan is appropriate.

> Bad: *"Our Q3 strategy is to increase pipeline by 40%."*  
> Fix: State the obstacle first — why is pipeline low, and what's causing it?

### 4. The Dog's Dinner
A long list of initiatives with no priority and no coherence. Usually the output of a brainstorm that was never filtered.

> Bad: *"We will improve SEO, launch a podcast, expand into EMEA, rebuild the mobile app, and grow partner referrals."*  
> Fix: Pick one or two things that reinforce each other. More initiatives dilute competitive punch.

## Five Principles for B2B SaaS Strategy

### 1. Make Trade-offs Explicit
Strategy requires choosing what *not* to do. If your strategy doesn't rule anything out, it isn't a strategy — it's a wish list.

For every initiative on the roadmap, ask: *What are we deprioritizing to do this? What customer segment are we saying no to?*

### 2. Focus on a Proximate Objective
Don't anchor strategy to a 5-year vision. Anchor it to a specific, achievable outcome in the next 12–18 months that you can win.

Kennedy didn't say "explore space." He said "put a man on the moon by the end of the decade" — a goal that was technically feasible and strategically sound because it played to a specific U.S. advantage.

> For B2B SaaS: *"Own the internal newsletter use case for companies with 500–5,000 employees by end of year"* is a proximate objective. *"Become the future of workplace communication"* is not.

### 3. Look for Chain-Link Problems
In SaaS, weak links kill otherwise good strategies. If your product is strong but onboarding is broken, fixing the product won't improve retention. You have to fix the weakest link first.

Before launching a growth initiative, ask: *What's the weakest link in the chain from awareness to renewal? Is the bottleneck acquisition, activation, conversion, or expansion?*

Fix the chain first. Pouring more into the top when the middle leaks is wasteful.

### 4. Ride the Wake, Don't Fight the Current
The best strategies take advantage of a shift already happening — they position you to benefit from change rather than fight it.

Ask: *What's changing in the buyer's world right now? What do they urgently need that they didn't need two years ago?*

Jobs didn't invent mobile computing. He saw the shift coming and focused everything on being first through that window with a complete product.

### 5. Diagnose Competitor Inertia
Large competitors are often your best asset. They're slow to change, structured around old assumptions, and competing internally as much as externally.

> For B2B SaaS: If you're up against a legacy platform, your strategy shouldn't be "beat them on features." It should be "move faster than they can respond" — on integrations, on UX, on time-to-value.

## What Good Marketing Strategy Looks Like

Marketing strategy is not a channel plan. It's the logic that connects your diagnosis to your market position.

**Good marketing strategy answers:**
- Who specifically is the buyer, and what problem are they urgently trying to solve?
- Why does this product solve it better than the alternatives (including doing nothing)?
- What do we need the buyer to *believe* before they'll convert?
- What is the single most important action we want the buyer to take next?

**Bad marketing strategy looks like:**
- A list of channels and content types without a connecting insight
- Messaging that describes features rather than the buyer's problem
- Campaigns aimed at "awareness" with no theory of how awareness converts to pipeline
- Separate strategies per channel with no coherent through-line

## Common Strategy Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | What It Looks Like | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Confusing activity for strategy | Long to-do lists, busy teams, no progress | Ask: what problem does this solve? |
| Chasing every opportunity | New initiative every quarter, no compounding | Commit to fewer things for longer |
| Ignoring your weakest link | Strong top-of-funnel, weak retention | Diagnose the bottleneck before scaling |
| Strategy by consensus | Bland plans that offend no one | Real strategy requires someone to say no |
| Extrapolating past success | "It worked before, do more of it" | Ask what has changed in the market |

---

## Quick Self-Check Before Publishing a Strategy Doc

1. Does it name a specific problem or obstacle?
2. Does it explain *how* — not just *what* — you'll achieve the goal?
3. Does it make at least one explicit trade-off?
4. Are the actions coherent — do they reinforce each other?
5. Could a new hire read this and know what to do *and* what not to do?

If yes to all five: you probably have a strategy.
