## Richard Rumelt's Opening: Why This Book

Thank you. It's a pleasure to be here at the London School of Economics. I lived in London for about six months while waiting to see if I would get tenure — a stressful time, but I did enjoy the city a lot.

This book, *Good Strategy Bad Strategy*, came out of me because of a growing frustration with the gap between what one thinks of as strategy and what actually passes for strategy in so many companies, in so many politicians' mouths, in so many government agencies, nonprofits, universities, and school systems. They say they have a strategy, but they don't. They have something else — something I call **bad strategy**.

Bad strategy is not the absence of strategy. It is an active force, a mistaken belief about how to think strategically and how to create strategies.

I also want to note: for those of you studying here at the London School of Economics, business strategy today is conceptualized mainly in terms of economics — competitive positioning within an industry, competitive advantage within industry structure. That view is not incorrect. But it is only one view. If we are going to talk about strategy for a nation state, a hospital, a government agency, a school, or a business firm, we need a language other than "competing within an industry," because that is only one possible regime.

## What Is Strategy: The Apple Example

### Apple in 1996

Let me take you back to 1996 and a company called Apple, which was almost bankrupt. This was the first time I had some interactions with Steve Jobs — who has since passed away. *Business Week* had a cover story basically writing the obituary for Apple. What had killed Apple was Windows 95: Microsoft had come out with a graphical user interface — mice and windows — which was originally the Apple innovation. Apple's market share had taken an extreme dive.

In October 1996, when Steve Jobs came back as CEO for no pay, Apple was two months from bankruptcy. That is how much cash it had, and that is how fast the cash burn was.

**The outside advice was:**
- Wall Street: do a deal — get bought by Sony, Sun, IBM, or Hewlett-Packard; you are basically out of business.
- Silicon Valley: sell your software to Microsoft, or invent something new and cool — do voice recognition or 3D.

The investment community expected him to do a deal.

### What Jobs Actually Did

What he did might be called Business 101. First, he did a deal with Microsoft — getting them to give Apple $150 million. How? Microsoft was under antitrust threat as a monopoly. The last thing Microsoft wanted was for Apple to go bankrupt, so the $150 million came in. That put bankruptcy off by about six months.

Then he cut ruthlessly:
- Apple had 16 models of desktop computer; he cut it down to **one**. As he told me: "My wife's sister asked me which computer to buy, and I looked at what we were selling and couldn't figure out which one to recommend. I'm the CEO — if I can't figure it out, how can a customer?"
- Dropped all printers and peripherals
- Canceled all software development; fired the software engineers
- Cut hardware engineers back to the bone
- Cut five of the six national retailers, keeping only CompUSA
- Moved all manufacturing to Taiwan
- Opened a web store to sell direct to customers

He did all of this in about four months. What do these actions add up to? Every single step was aimed at **survival**. They were about to go bankrupt. No R&D, no product development, cut back to a core that could survive.

People were surprised. Why? Because they do not expect to see anyone actually perform this kind of focused action. We expect our leaders to espouse fluffy goals and try to do everything: "We're going to have better education, better healthcare, better social services, faster railways — we're going to have it all." Down that road lies nothing as strategy. Strategy is about focus.

### Jobs's Long-Term Thinking

I sat down with him a year later and said, "Okay, you've survived. You've got 4% of the desktop market; Windows-Intel machines have still won. What's your long-term strategy?"

He looked at me and said: **"I'm going to wait for the next big thing."**

I had just spent six months interviewing about 75 executives in electronics — division managers and CEOs — and I had asked each one how the most successful player in their industry got to be that successful. They all had basically the same story: something had changed, some new technology appeared, some new window of opportunity opened, and that company was the first to jump through it.

Then I'd ask: "Okay, so what is *your* strategy?" And they would give me a long list — forming network relationships, moving software to firmware, doing 360-degree feedback, transferring best practices. They were doing everything *except* what they said success came from, which was waiting for a window of opportunity to open and then jumping through it full force.

That is what Jobs was saying: wait for the next big thing. It was an intelligent answer in that industry at that moment. The next big thing for him was the iPod, then the iPhone, then the iPad. In each case, a window of opportunity opened and he went after it with full force and full focus. All of the pieces — software, hardware, manufacturing, distribution, design — were focused on pushing that product through to excellence. **That is strategy.**

## Bad Strategy: What It Looks Like

### Example 1: The Graphics Arts Company

A man named Chad Logan hired me to take a look at his company. He said, "I need your help with strategy." He had inherited a graphics arts company from his uncle, and he told me: "We have a plan — I call it the 20/20 Plan: 20% growth in revenues a year and 20% after-tax profits."

I said, "Those are great goals, but what is the *strategy*? How is a fairly ordinary graphics arts company going to do this?"

He said, "One thing I learned as a football player is you have to have the will to win. We're just going to push until we achieve this."

They actually had a plaque on the wall. Their strategy was:

> *"We're going to be the graphics arts firm of choice. We will delight our customers. We'll grow our revenue 20% a year. We'll maintain a profit margin of 20% or better. We have a culture of commitment. We'll foster an honest and open work environment. We'll support the broader community in which we serve."*

There is nothing here about strategy. It is all puff — things we think are nice, plus results we would like to achieve. **That is bad strategy.**

### Example 2: The Big Corporate Strategy Meeting

I attended a major strategy meeting at a large corporation — two to three hundred senior managers from around the world. They had a specially produced movie of the company's products, specially commissioned music, and the CEO gave a speech about the new strategy. The strategy was: "We're going for Global Leadership. We're going for growth. We're going for shareholder return."

Then there were breakout sessions where individual managers had to "buy in" to this vision. Where is the strategy? It is not there.

They did have an annual strategic planning system — a three-year plan that started each September and finished each June, with the first year becoming next year's budget. What it actually was: a forecast of the economy, a forecast of revenues, a forecast of profits. It was a bunch of financial results. **A strategy is not what you hope your performance will be. Strategy is about how you are going to get there.**

### The Origins of the Term "Bad Strategy"

I developed the phrase "bad strategy" at a National Security conference in 2007. We were reviewing classified and unclassified documents — there were only nine of us in the room, including a former head of the CIA and a former head of the Department of Defense. Their big complaint was: "There is no strategy." What they got were national goals — end terrorism, stop weapons of mass destruction — but then nothing about *how*. **It did not used to be that way.**

## Good Strategy: Real Examples

### Lou Gerstner's Turnaround of IBM

Before pulling out your antidepressants — there are plenty of good strategies out there. Consider Lou Gerstner's turnaround of IBM.

When he walked in, in 1993, IBM's mainframe business had collapsed and the PC business had commoditized. The Wall Street and investment banking advisors were all prepared to split IBM into pieces. The idea was that the computer industry had fragmented — there are now operating system companies, memory companies, hard disk companies, etc. — so IBM itself should fragment to match.

Gerstner said no. If you fragment, you lose the base of competence and the knowledge about all the different customers in the world. What IBM had to do instead was **stop being an integrated product company and become a solutions provider** — even if the solution was a non-IBM product.

To do that took eight or nine years. Most of the challenges were organizational. He fired 75,000 people, rehired many people, made huge reductions in internal processes, outsourced many processes, and put enormous pressure on the organization to become faster and higher quality — all focused around that core change. He understood the problem and devised an approach to dealing with it. **That is a strategy.**

## The Four Hallmarks of Bad Strategy

### 1. It's All Goals — No How

The classic hockey-stick graph: profits down, then a plan to go up. Consider International Harvester's strategic plan in 1979 — they projected a dramatic profit increase over the next five years. In 1980 and 1981, they went bankrupt.

CEOs will say, "We had a great plan, but we had problems implementing it," suggesting that if only people had worked harder, the numbers would have been met. What this plan left out was the elephant in the elevator: International Harvester at that point had the worst labor relations of probably any company in the US — possibly the world. The site of the Haymarket Riots, terrible work rules, wage costs higher than anyone else in the industry.

Given that environment, their strategy should have been to deal with that problem. Instead, they did a ridiculous financial forecast and ignored their basic problem. **Strategy that is all goals is bad strategy.**

### 2. It's All Fluff

A bank I became acquainted with stated that their strategy was "customer-oriented disintermediation." They are a retail bank — of course they are customer-oriented. And disintermediation means they are a bank. So the strategy was "we are a bank" — but they fluffed it up.

**Fluff is not strategy.** People confuse the idea of strategy with abstract words.

### 3. No Problem Is Defined

A lot of bad strategies are all about the future — where we hope to get — but they never address: what are the challenges? What essential forces are we struggling with?

Every organization, every country, every business, every nonprofit, every school has a challenge of some kind. Unless you identify it, focus on it, think about it, and analyze it, you cannot develop a strategy. And after you develop a strategy, other people cannot evaluate whether it is good or bad unless you have stated what problem it is trying to solve.

Take the US job strategy question. Why aren't there jobs? What happened to economic growth? Unless you figure that out, you cannot have a jobs strategy. The jobs strategy has to treat the disease.

You cannot stimulate an economy every month forever. We have been stimulating the US economy every quarter for 11 years. When you give someone amphetamines every quarter for 11 years, they become comatose. And when you drop interest rates down to 1% and keep them there for four or five years, you get huge property and asset bubbles.

One of the things that has happened is we do not have technologies that are employing large numbers of people. All through the 20th century, every invention created huge amounts of employment — railroads, airplanes, electricity, chemicals, television, computers, automobiles. Google employs 10,000 people. YouTube? Hardly anybody. Our new technologies, while exciting, do not employ anyone, and the old technologies are being shifted abroad. **That is a problem. When you begin to identify the problem, you begin to think about strategies for coping with it.**

### 4. It's a Dog's Dinner

A "dog's dinner" is a long list of things — what committees produce. Seventy-five items: clean up the sidewalks, fix the seats, that kind of thing. That is not strategy.

## What Good Strategy Is: The Kernel

Good strategy has something I call a **kernel** — the center of a seed from which a strategy grows. The kernel has three components:

### 1. Diagnosis
What is the nature of the challenge? Why is economic growth flatlined? Why is there a European debt crisis? Why are 40% of the kids dropping out of school in the Los Angeles School District?

You will find, over and over, that people put forward ideas about what to do with no diagnosis. "Let's hire better teachers." "Let's spend more on books." But wait — is that the problem? Is the problem that we don't have enough books? You have to do the diagnosis.

A good strategist is a bit like a scientist — working with empirical data and forming hypotheses. A social scientist will often diagnose a problem in a way that has maximum causal explanation but no levers for change — nothing you can do about it. A good strategist will try to find a diagnosis that suggests: "Here is something you can do to change things."

### 2. Guiding Policy
How are we going to approach this problem? What is our basic approach? This is a decision. Your diagnosis is also a decision — you choose a diagnosis.

### 3. Coherent Actions
The third element. If all three are not present, you do not have a strategy as powerful as it could be.

**A strategy is a coherent mix of policy and action designed to surmount a difficult, usually high-stakes challenge.** It is not an industry analysis, not a goal, not a wish list.

## Principles of Good Strategy

### 1. Diagnose the Challenge

As discussed. Whether it is the Greek debt crisis (governments borrowing to buy votes, banks lending for a half-percent yield advantage and expecting a bailout), or domestic economic stagnation — you must identify the actual problem.

On Greece: the problem will not be solved by bailing out banks or bailing out Greece. It is a systemic behavior problem of national governments within the EU itself, and of banks. More money is just a palliative — like taking aspirin for cancer.

### 2. Make Your Actions Coherent — Focus Is the Heart of Strategy

**The Battle of Cannae, 216 BC.** Hannibal, with about 55,000 troops, faced Romans with somewhere between 80,000 and 120,000 soldiers. Hannibal arranged his troops in an arc — Spanish and Gallic soldiers in the center, Carthaginian heavy infantry on the flanks, cavalry on both sides.

When the two armies came together, the bow of Hannibal's line began to flatten, then bow inward. The Romans, seeing the center retreating, thought they were winning — they pushed together in the center to break through. The center bowed further back. But the Carthaginian heavy infantry on the flanks was not engaged, just hanging around. Then the center stopped, and those Spanish and Gallic soldiers suddenly turned and held. Reinforcements came in from the side. The cavalry — unexpectedly — came in from the rear.

The Romans were encircled. Now the numbers had been reversed. Hannibal had more effective fighters than the Romans. Most Romans were facing the wrong way, jammed together so hard that many could not lift their weapons. We think 55,000 to 60,000 Romans died that day — more than any other battle in history, before or since.

Hannibal did this over and over again for ten years. He is called the father of strategy because through these battles he taught the Romans to think strategically instead of just charging ahead.

What is the lesson at a high level of abstraction? It was a **coordination in time and space that was unanticipated, clever, and adroit** — a design where the Romans' own strength, their own courage, their own mobility, was turned against them. Strategy exploits the predictable nature of the world — whether it is your enemy, the weather, or business cycles. You coordinate your forces around what you are trying to accomplish. The competitive advantage of strategy is that focus, that coordination.

### 3. Insight

There is no way around it. Strategy is about insight. It is not about looking at a matrix or a graph and picking your strategy; it is not about the eight boxes. Any more than a great film director uses four preset plots. Anyone trying to sell you that it's a formula is selling you a fake. **It is an insight.**

### 4. Create Proximate Objectives

Strategy is not about the far future, but the way we get to the future is by doing something now. A **proximate objective** is one that is feasible — something we can actually achieve in the near term. Near term: one year, 18 months, two years maximum. It does not have to be that close, but it has to be pretty feasible.

Take Kennedy's goal of putting someone on the moon by the end of the 1960s. This is often held up as a "big hairy audacious goal" or a "giant leap into the unknown." It was neither. We knew how to put people on the moon — technically, it was expensive but not mysterious. The real problem to solve was the throttleable rocket for landing (since there's no air, no parachutes).

Kennedy asked the Vice President what to do to beat the Russians. With no answer, he wrote to von Braun, who replied: "If we look beyond the rockets we have today and build much larger ones, then because we have more industrial base, we can probably build that rocket faster — and that rocket could take people to the moon and back. That is a goal we can beat them at."

One month later, Kennedy gave his speech. It was feasible, and highly strategic: by looking at what a very large rocket would require, he identified where the United States could win. **That is the kind of proximate objective that drives strategy.**

### 5. Ride the Wake

Great companies come out of technical change — changes in the world that you take advantage of and ride. Companies like Cisco and Microsoft were able to ride the wave of microprocessors upsetting the entire computing and telecommunications landscape.

For Apple, the wave Steve Jobs was riding was a **shift from the desktop to the mobile platform**.

### 6. Chain Links

Chain-link situations are where, to achieve excellence, you need a bunch of things all working. Any one of them failing means the excellence is not there — just like a chain is only as strong as its weakest link. It is not additive: if one factor is below standard, adding more of the others does not compensate.

This is also why it is hard to copy: when five or six things are all working well together, that combination is very difficult to replicate. And it is why efforts to fix only one part of a chain-link system do not repay their investment — you have to fix several parts together to get a result.

Developing countries illustrate the trap: better ports don't help without better roads; better ports and roads don't help if pirates are stealing cargo; fix those and you still face government corruption; fix that and cheap imports put local producers out of business. Problem after problem has to be addressed together for investment to pay off.

### 7. Expect Entropy and Inertia

Good strategy understands that the real world is a mess. Competitors are not alert. Your own company may be your biggest challenge. Things are hard to change.

**General Motors** illustrates entropy. In 1919, Alfred Sloan created a brilliant product policy: each division — Chevrolet, Oakland/Pontiac, Buick, Oldsmobile, Cadillac — had its own distinct price category. This concept catapulted GM into being the world's largest industrial corporation by 1950. In 1956, you could walk down any American suburban street and tell who lived there by the car in the driveway.

By 2008, as General Motors went bankrupt, its product line had no clear pattern. They had 15 products at the $22,000 price point. How does that happen after years and years of corporate strategy?

It happens because the original plan gets mushed over time. The Buick division, wanting to sell more units and earn bigger bonuses, pushes headquarters to allow a lower-priced car. Headquarters' job is to say no — just like parents when 13-year-olds want beer at the party — but over time it's hard to say no. Buick starts making cheaper cars, Chevrolet starts making expensive cars, everything blurs together, and the company starts competing with itself across all its own brands.

That is **entropy** — and anticipating it is part of good strategy.

## The Nvidia Case Study: Strategy in Action

In the early 1980s, 3D graphics were used for flight simulators and niche applications. In the early 1990s, it became clear that 3D graphics could be done on personal computers — but no one knew for what. The speculation was virtual city tours, surgical education, architect renderings.

What actually happened: 3D graphics was pulled into the market by violent video games. *Doom* (1995) was the catalyst. If your video card was slow, you got killed constantly by your friends playing over the internet, so teenage boys would buy whatever accelerator gave them an edge.

In 1996, **3dfx** brought out the first effective PC graphics accelerator. SGI, which had invented many of the foundational concepts, ignored this burgeoning market — their CEO from Hewlett-Packard was after workstations and set a goal of 50% growth per year.

**Nvidia** brought out a card aimed at this market, but it failed — wrong technology. They went back to the drawing board, formed a strategy committee, and arrived at a diagnosis and plan:

- **Diagnosis:** Gamers will pay for almost any performance we can deliver. The industry works on an 18-month cycle (Moore's Law as industrial machine). 3D graphics is currently spread over multiple chips and software. We can push this onto one chip through integration, advancing performance two to three times faster than Moore's Law. That lets us ship a new chip every **six months** instead of every 18.
- **The strategic logic:** If you release every six months while competitors release every 18 months, you are at the technology frontier more frequently — on average, higher performance in the market, more buzz.

**What they did to make it work (coherent actions):**

- Built **three overlapping design teams**, each on an 18-month cycle but staggered six months apart — so a new design shipped every six months.
- Built the **world's largest chip simulation facility** — to simulate electrical characteristics in software so there would be no rework when chips came back from fabricators. This eliminated the devastating delays of chips that did not work on first run.
- Built **complex emulation facilities** — so software development could begin before the chip was finished, rather than waiting for the chip to write the software.
- **Went around board makers** — instead of selling chips to established board makers who would add their software on their timeline, they found no-name Asian board makers willing to use Nvidia's chips and Nvidia's software. They did not want to get into the board business, but they could not afford the delay.

The result: Nvidia's graphics chips became more complex than Intel CPUs around 1999-2000 and continued to be the most complex chips in personal computers. The world's fastest computer built a year and a half ago (at the time of the talk) used Nvidia graphics chips.

3dfx went bankrupt. Intel tried twice to enter 3D graphics and failed both times — a large, technically competent company, but unable to shift from an 18-month cycle to a six-month rapid evolution cycle in what was, for Intel, a small market.

## Q&A Session

### On Strategy Under Uncertainty (Questioner: Mr. Stefanon, Oxford)

**Q:** How can you focus on something when you don't know enough in the diagnosis phase?

**Rumelt:** A good strategist is a bit like a scientist — working with empirical data and forming hypotheses. A strategy is fundamentally a hypothesis about what is going to work. We'd like a hypothesis we're sure will work, but we rarely have that certainty.

What we try to do is develop some tests, some experimental data that tells us more about the nature of the problem. Companies with multiple countries, outlets, stores, or initiatives can see which ones work better and move more quickly. In government and policy areas, collecting sufficient information to analyze the nature of the problem is costly.

The worst strategic situations are where there is huge uncertainty and only one try — then you need an accurate diagnosis, a good plan, and perfect execution, with no intermediate improvement possible. As strategists we try to create situations where we can make incremental improvements over time rather than one big leap.

### On Recommendations for the US Economy (Questioner: Nisha, NSE student)

**Q:** What would you recommend the United States do to get out of the current economic mess?

**Rumelt:**

First, the **government has to become more efficient**. It is incredibly inefficient, which has created a large group of voters who do not trust it to do anything. They see it spend a million dollars a year to put one soldier in Afghanistan. They see it throw trillions at banks. They see that after Katrina, the federal government couldn't get trucks there because drivers didn't have permits to cross state lines. This is incompetence and inefficiency. The government should provide healthcare to everyone, but does anyone trust it to do that in a way that isn't politically skewed or horribly inefficient?

Second — and this is the basic thing — **the dollar is too high**. We have priced American labor out of the world market with a high dollar. My economist friends can't fully explain why it is so high given interest rates at zero, but that is the thing that has to be tackled. It keeps America from competing effectively in exports.

Infrastructure, fast trains, education — all potentially useful, but none of those seem solvable by a government that is inefficient and does things totally politically rather than in a reasonable way. The two key problems: the dollar is too high, and the government is inefficient.

### On Good Strategy at State and Local Level (Questioner from audience)

**Q:** Can you give an illustration of good strategies currently being employed at the state or local level? What kind of political leadership does it take?

**Rumelt:** It takes imaginative political leaders with genuine political power — the power to do things that aren't just what your supporters want.

New Mexico is an interesting example. The previous governor put in a set of policies to move film production there. What is interesting is they didn't just put in a tax break. They also put in college courses on film technology, film editing; they assembled studios where that work could be done. So there was focus — four, five, six different actions and policies, all in a fairly short period, all aimed at making New Mexico not just a filming location because of the settings, but a place where the skills would be developed and retained.

That is a pretty clean strategy. Fairly simple, and it is working reasonably well.

### On Whether Great Leaders Are Required (Questioner: Alex, from Russia)

**Q:** Does an organization or a country need a great leader to have a good strategy?

**Rumelt:** Hopefully, nation states don't need complex strategies unless they are going to war. They should basically provide the hygiene factors that allow everybody to get on with life — decent infrastructure, a certain amount of education, things like that.

The trouble with "great leaders" is that sometimes they're horrible and sometimes they're good. It's like monarchs: there are moments when monarchs create golden ages because they're long-lived and very smart. But once you commit to monarchy, you're also going to have some terrible monarchs who take you down into hell.

What strategy really is is **an imposition on a system**. If we look at a country or organization and ask what it will do over the next three to five years if left to its own devices, we can roughly forecast it. Strategy is to change that — to create coherence between disparate pieces.

Nokia was at one point the leader in smartphones, then it began to let each piece of the business do its own thing. Now it is flailing. It is not so much that you need a great leader as you need **coherence** — and the people involved to believe that coherence is necessary. It is like a symphony orchestra: there has to be some tempo set. It doesn't mean it is an autocracy, but something has to be happening beyond everybody doing their own thing. That is the nature of strategy.

When we form organizations or nation states, the separate pieces and geographies naturally go off and pursue their own interests — and that is freedom, and we like that. But certain things are lost when we do that. Strategy is about identifying the key things we do not want to lose and getting coherence around them. Very important in military affairs, less important in national affairs, very important in fast-growing businesses. Pulling in different directions dilutes the competitive punch.

### On Strategy Consulting (Questioner from audience)

**Q:** What is your view of the strategy consulting profession and its contribution?

**Rumelt:** Strategy consulting is all over the place. There are high-quality firms and high-quality people. And then there are scams.

I recently saw an advertisement for a "60-Minute Strategy" seminar: Step 1 — imagine the best possible thing that can happen. Step 2 — write down a plan around that. Step 3 — give it to senior management. That is a scam.

People want to believe there is a magical way to achieve success. The books on how to succeed: "Secret Number One — aim high, if you aim low you'll never wind up high. Number Two — never give up. Number Three — don't think too much so you won't question Numbers One and Two." That could be a bestseller. But in terms of value to someone with a difficult challenge, it is about as useful as carving your company name on a tree and burying a dead cat under it. It is superstition, and yet the human heart has an affinity for it.

### On Strategy in SMEs Serving Developing Markets (Questioner: small business owner serving East/West Africa)

**Q:** What is your opinion about developing strategies for SMEs servicing very asymmetrical markets?

**Rumelt:** On the developing world problem more broadly — I am 68 years old and developing countries have been developing my whole life. We are beginning to need a different word.

The issues are **chain-linked**: when you fix one thing, it's not good enough. Better ports don't help without better roads. Better ports and roads don't help if there are criminals stealing goods. Fix that, and it doesn't help if the government is corrupt. Fix the corruption, ports, roads, and safety — and it still doesn't help if imported products are putting local people out of business.

This is why efforts to fix one part of a chain-link system do not repay their investment cost. You have to fix a bunch of parts to get a result. That is the core difficulty — not just for your business, but for the broader environment in which it operates. There are so many different pieces that must be put together for things to work.

### On Whether Good Strategy Always Leads to Success (Questioner: student, LSE IDEAS)

**Q:** Does a strategy that focuses resources on a correctly identified problem guarantee success? Do you equate good strategy with success and bad strategy with failure?

**Rumelt:** No. Bad strategy can succeed and good strategy can fail. The world is uncertain.

Companies sometimes succeed from luck. Groupon's strategy, from a business policy viewpoint, seems to be to get a lot of money from stock investors — it does not look like a sustainable business. Maybe that is the strategy. But it is flaky.

The arc of companies and countries is that they usually do something right. Then they begin to succeed, grow, and have great things happen — and they get into the habit of extrapolating. Strategy becomes looking at next year's increment of growth. You lose the habits of critical evaluation because you do not need them when the rain is falling and the sun is shining.

Then, 10 or 15 years down the road, suddenly there is a blight and a drought — and you have forgotten how to deal with adversity. You have forgotten to put grain aside for the bad years. Remember the Bible — seven fat years and seven lean years. This is not new wisdom, but we forget it when things are good.

This is **the cycle in the affairs of people**: good times lead eventually to sloth and decadence, which leads to bad times, which leads — maybe — to a strengthening and return of good times. Or maybe it just leads to Easter Island.

### On Quantitative Easing and Central Bank Strategy (Questioner: Luke)

**Q:** Do you think central banks governing bodies have a strategy for dealing with the economic crisis, with QE1, QE2, and presumably more to come?

**Rumelt:** I think it is a fantasy. They started out throwing money at banks because they formed an erroneous belief that the problem was that banks weren't lending. It is now clear — and was clear then — that the problem was not that banks weren't lending. People didn't want to borrow. So throwing money into banks to increase liquidity didn't accomplish anything.

Then they put interest rates down to zero, which hurts people like me who saved all their lives and can no longer earn anything. What they wanted was for people to take their money out of safe things, put it into risky things, drive up asset prices, and then feel richer and go out and spend. That hasn't worked either.

**Central banks should stop trying to fine-tune economies.** And they should not bail out banks. If a bank makes a lot of bad loans, the bank should be nationalized, the managers fired, it should be recapitalized and spun off. The Swedes did this — with great skill and speed.

At the time I suggested that the US use Guantanamo Bay to take banks through rapid bankruptcies — I was rather quickly shown out of the room. But the fundamental problem is not the Federal Reserve or central banks. It is the legal structure, at least in the United States, which prevents you from doing anything quickly to take a bank through bankruptcy and reconstitute it. It would require 10 to 15 years of legal fighting.

Certainly banks should not be bailed out. And splitting them into small pieces is not the answer to "too big to fail," because much of the risk is counterparty risk — the more counterparties there are, the more risk there is. What should be limited are the incentive structures that allow people to become extraordinarily wealthy by taking hidden risks. Banks and insurance companies share the same pathology: you can always make current results look better by taking a hidden risk that is under the table. People do it, and they walk home with $30 million bonuses.

## Closing Summary

**Rumelt's final summary:**

- **Bad strategy** is fluff — "here's where we're going to go" without "here's how we're going to get there." It fails to define the nature of the challenge, so no one can assess whether the plan is good or bad, because no one knows what it is trying to deal with.

- **Good strategy** focuses energy. It does not disperse effort in all directions. It focuses particularly on a **proximate objective** — something critical that can be accomplished in the near future.
